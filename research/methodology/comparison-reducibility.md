# Comparison Reducibility

## Purpose

Determine whether comparison is a primitive methodological concept or whether it is reducible to more fundamental concepts.

The objective is to identify whether comparison should remain a foundational concept within Project FAR.

---

# Motivation

The previous investigation suggested that evaluation may be reducible to comparison.

Before accepting comparison as primitive, every reasonable reduction shall be investigated.

---

# Central Question

Can comparison be completely explained using more fundamental concepts?

---

# Candidate Reduction 1

Comparison as Distinguishability.

Question:

Can two entities be compared unless they are first distinguishable?

Evaluation:

No.

If two entities cannot be distinguished, comparison is impossible.

Result:

Promising.

---

# Candidate Reduction 2

Comparison as Relation.

Question:

Is comparison merely a relation?

Evaluation:

A relation may exist without determining similarity or difference.

Comparison appears to establish a relation rather than be identical with one.

Result:

Rejected.

---

# Candidate Reduction 3

Comparison as Ordering.

Question:

Can every comparison be expressed as an ordering?

Evaluation:

No.

Many comparisons determine equivalence rather than order.

Result:

Rejected.

---

# Pattern Analysis

Every investigated comparison presupposes distinguishability.

Distinguishability does not necessarily require comparison.

One may distinguish entities without comparing them.

---

# Competing Hypotheses

## H1

Comparison is primitive.

---

## H2

Comparison consists of relating distinguishable entities according to one or more criteria.

---

# Evaluation

Current evidence favors H2.

Comparison appears to require distinguishability.

Distinguishability therefore appears more fundamental.

---

# Decision Criteria

Comparison is reducible if every comparison presupposes distinguishability while distinguishability can exist independently of comparison.

---

# Provisional Conclusion

Current evidence suggests that comparison is not primitive.

Instead, comparison appears to require prior distinguishability.

Whether distinguishability itself is primitive remains unresolved.

---

# Remaining Questions

Future investigations should determine:

- whether distinguishability is primitive;
- whether criteria are primitive;
- whether representations require distinguishability;
- whether distinguishability can be formally characterized.

---

# Current Status

The investigation remains active.

Comparison remains a reducible candidate concept.
