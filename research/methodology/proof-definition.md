# Proof Definition

## Purpose

Determine the necessary and sufficient conditions under which a reasoning process constitutes a proof within Project FAR.

The objective is to distinguish proofs from explanations, arguments, demonstrations, and unsupported assertions.

---

# Motivation

Project FAR relies upon proofs to establish:

- dependency claims;
- theorem claims;
- framework properties;
- methodological conclusions.

The concept of proof must therefore be formally defined.

---

# Central Question

What distinguishes a proof from other forms of reasoning?

---

# Candidate 1

Explanation.

Question:

Is every explanation a proof?

Evaluation:

No.

An explanation may increase understanding without establishing the truth of its conclusion.

Result:

Rejected.

---

# Candidate 2

Argument.

Question:

Is every argument a proof?

Evaluation:

No.

Arguments may contain unsupported premises, invalid inferences, or unresolved objections.

Result:

Rejected.

---

# Candidate 3

Demonstration.

Question:

Is every demonstration a proof?

Evaluation:

Not necessarily.

A demonstration may illustrate a claim without establishing its necessity.

Result:

Rejected.

---

# Candidate 4

Justified Derivation.

Question:

Does every proof consist of a justified derivation from accepted premises according to accepted reasoning rules?

Evaluation:

This candidate accounts for:

- mathematical proofs;
- logical proofs;
- dependency proofs;
- methodological proofs.

Result:

Supported.

---

# Pattern Analysis

The investigated proofs possess common characteristics.

Each contains:

- explicitly identified premises;
- explicitly identified reasoning rules;
- a derivation;
- a conclusion;
- justification for every inferential step.

Activities lacking one or more of these characteristics fail to qualify as proofs.

---

# Candidate Definition

A proof is a justified derivation of a conclusion from explicitly identified premises according to explicitly identified reasoning rules.

This definition remains provisional.

---

# Consequences

If accepted:

- every proof within Project FAR becomes structurally analyzable;
- proof validity can be independently evaluated;
- proof checking becomes possible;
- dependency proofs become a special case of general proofs.

---

# Remaining Questions

Future investigations should determine:

- what constitutes justification;
- whether every proof possesses minimal structure;
- whether proofs admit composition;
- whether proof identity can be formally defined.

---

# Current Status

The investigation remains active.

No final definition of proof has yet been established.
