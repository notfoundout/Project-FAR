# FARO Discovery Investigation

## Identifier

FARO-023

---

# Title

Necessity of a Reasoning Calculus

---

## Status

In Progress

---

# Purpose

Determine whether every reasoning process necessarily presupposes an explicitly defined reasoning calculus.

---

# Dependencies

- FARO-001 through FARO-022

---

# Central Question

Can reasoning exist without an explicitly defined reasoning calculus?

---

# Background

The previous investigation identified conformity to a reasoning calculus as a candidate minimal condition for reasoning.

This investigation attempts to falsify that hypothesis.

---

# Null Hypothesis

Every reasoning process requires an explicitly defined reasoning calculus.

---

# Alternative Hypothesis

Reasoning may occur before a reasoning calculus is explicitly defined.

---

# Test Case 1

An individual attempts to solve a novel problem.

They have never studied formal logic.

They compare possibilities, reject alternatives, and eventually reach a conclusion.

Question:

Has reasoning occurred?

Observation:

Intuitively, yes.

No explicit reasoning calculus has been stated.

---

# Test Case 2

A scientist investigates an unfamiliar phenomenon.

Methods evolve during the investigation.

Question:

Must the complete reasoning calculus exist before reasoning begins?

Observation:

No.

The investigation itself may contribute to the refinement of reasoning procedures.

---

# Test Case 3

A child compares two explanations.

The child has never formalized rules of inference.

Question:

Has reasoning occurred?

Observation:

Yes.

An explicit calculus is absent.

---

# Analysis

The investigated cases suggest that reasoning may precede explicit formalization.

However, they do not establish that reasoning occurs without any operational regularity.

The absence of an explicit calculus does not imply the absence of an implicit one.

---

# Emerging Hypothesis

Every reasoning process may conform to a reasoning calculus.

The calculus need not be explicitly represented.

Instead, it may be implicit within the operational behavior itself.

---

# Competing Hypotheses

## H1

Reasoning requires an explicit reasoning calculus.

---

## H2

Reasoning requires only operational regularity.

An explicit calculus is one representation of that regularity.

---

# Evaluation

Current evidence challenges H1.

The distinction between explicit and implicit calculi appears significant.

Operational behavior may precede formal specification.

---

# Decision Criteria

If reasoning consistently exhibits operational regularity even when no explicit calculus has been formulated, explicitness is not a necessary condition for reasoning.

---

# Provisional Conclusion

Current evidence suggests that reasoning does not require an explicitly represented reasoning calculus.

Instead, reasoning may require only that operations exhibit coherent regularity capable of later formalization.

---

# Remaining Questions

Future investigations should determine:

- whether implicit calculi can always be formalized;
- whether multiple explicit calculi can describe the same operational behavior;
- whether operational regularity is the true primitive.

---

# Current Status

The investigation remains active.

The necessity of explicit reasoning calculi has not been established.
