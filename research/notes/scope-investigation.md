# Scope Investigation

## Purpose

This document records the investigation of the scope of Project FAR.

Its purpose is to determine precisely which reasoning processes Project FAR is intended to represent, analyze, and evaluate.

No conclusion recorded in this document should be regarded as part of the canonical theory until it has been incorporated into the formal documentation.

---

# Motivation

The scope of Project FAR determines:

- the required expressive power of the framework;
- the sufficiency of the primitive architecture;
- the applicability of theoretical results; and
- the validity of claims concerning universality.

Accordingly, the scope must be established before the architecture can be fully justified.

---

# Research Questions

The investigation seeks to answer the following questions.

1. What is a reasoning process?

2. What distinguishes reasoning from other forms of information processing?

3. What kinds of reasoning fall within the intended scope of Project FAR?

4. What kinds of reasoning are intentionally excluded?

5. What minimum characteristics must every reasoning process possess?

6. Does Project FAR describe reasoning itself or only explicit representations of reasoning?

7. What assumptions about reasoning are required by the framework?

---

# Current Working Objective

The current objective of Project FAR is to develop a universal framework capable of representing, analyzing, and evaluating every reasoning process within its stated scope.

The precise boundaries of that scope remain under investigation.

---

# Candidate Scope

Current discussions suggest that Project FAR is intended to encompass reasoning processes including, but not necessarily limited to:

- mathematical reasoning;
- scientific reasoning;
- legal reasoning;
- historical reasoning;
- philosophical reasoning;
- political reasoning;
- engineering reasoning;
- everyday decision-making;
- formal proofs;
- informal arguments.

Whether additional forms of reasoning belong within the framework remains an open question.

---

# Candidate Exclusions

The following possibilities require investigation.

- Pure sensory perception.
- Emotional experience.
- Reflexive behavior.
- Random processes.
- Biological processes lacking representational reasoning.
- Physical causation independent of reasoning.

These exclusions remain provisional.

# Investigation 1 — What is a Reasoning Process?

## Status

In Progress

---

## Research Question

What is a reasoning process?

---

## Motivation

Project FAR cannot establish its scope until the object of study is precisely identified.

Accordingly, the concept of a reasoning process must be investigated before determining what falls within the scope of the framework.

---

## Candidate Definitions

### Candidate A

A reasoning process is any process that produces a conclusion.

#### Assessment

Too broad.

Many physical processes produce outcomes without reasoning.

Rejected.

---

### Candidate B

A reasoning process is any process that transforms information.

#### Assessment

Too broad.

Compression algorithms, encryption, and random shuffling transform information without necessarily performing reasoning.

Rejected.

---

### Candidate C

A reasoning process is a process that transforms representations according to rules.

#### Assessment

Closer, but still incomplete.

Many computational transformations satisfy this definition without performing reasoning.

Further refinement required.

Status:

Under Investigation.

---

### Candidate D

A reasoning process is a goal-directed process that transforms representations according to a reasoning calculus within the context of an investigation.

#### Assessment

This candidate closely resembles the current primitive architecture.

However, adopting it now would risk circularity because it defines reasoning in terms of concepts whose necessity has not yet been established.

Status:

Deferred.

---

## Observations

Current investigations suggest that reasoning involves more than representation and transformation.

A successful definition must distinguish reasoning from arbitrary computation while remaining sufficiently general to encompass every reasoning process within the intended scope of Project FAR.

---

## Open Questions

- Is reasoning necessarily goal-directed?
- Must every reasoning process possess an investigation?
- Is a reasoning calculus required?
- Is representation sufficient, or is interpretation also necessary?
- What distinguishes reasoning from general computation?

---

## Current Conclusion

No satisfactory definition has yet been identified.

The investigation remains open.

# Investigation 2 — What Distinguishes Reasoning from Computation?

## Status

In Progress

---

## Research Question

What distinguishes a reasoning process from a general computational process?

---

## Motivation

If every computational process qualifies as reasoning, then the scope of Project FAR becomes unnecessarily broad.

Conversely, if reasoning is defined too narrowly, legitimate reasoning processes may be excluded.

A precise distinction is therefore required.

---

## Candidate Criterion A

Reasoning produces conclusions.

### Assessment

Insufficient.

Many computational processes produce outputs without performing reasoning.

Examples include:

- sorting algorithms;
- compression algorithms;
- encryption;
- random number generation.

Rejected.

---

## Candidate Criterion B

Reasoning transforms representations.

### Assessment

Insufficient.

Many computational processes transform representations without performing reasoning.

Transformation alone is not a distinguishing characteristic.

Rejected.

---

## Candidate Criterion C

Reasoning transforms representations according to rules.

### Assessment

Insufficient.

Every deterministic algorithm follows rules.

Rule-governed transformation alone does not distinguish reasoning from computation.

Rejected.

---

## Candidate Criterion D

Reasoning justifies transitions between representations.

### Assessment

Promising.

Unlike arbitrary computation, reasoning appears to require that transitions be justified according to an accepted reasoning calculus.

The distinguishing feature may therefore be normative rather than procedural.

Status:

Under Investigation.

---

## Observations

Current analysis suggests that computation describes what transformations occur.

Reasoning may additionally require that those transformations satisfy explicit standards of admissibility or justification.

If correct, reasoning constitutes a specialized form of computation rather than computation in general.

This hypothesis remains subject to further investigation.

---

## Open Questions

- Is justification necessary for every reasoning process?
- Can heuristic reasoning satisfy this criterion?
- Can incorrect reasoning still be reasoning?
- Does reasoning require truth preservation, or only rule-governed admissibility?
- Is reasoning fundamentally normative?

---

## Current Conclusion

No definitive distinction has yet been established.

However, current evidence suggests that justification may distinguish reasoning from general computation.

Further investigation is required.

# Investigation 3 — Is Reasoning Necessarily Representational?

## Status

In Progress

---

## Research Question

Must every reasoning process involve representations?

---

## Motivation

The current primitive architecture begins with Representation.

If reasoning can occur without representations, then the architecture is fundamentally incomplete.

Conversely, if representation is necessary for every reasoning process within the scope of Project FAR, then Representation is justified as a foundational primitive.

---

## Candidate Hypothesis A

Every reasoning process necessarily involves one or more representations.

---

## Candidate Hypothesis B

Some reasoning processes occur without representations.

---

## Candidate Counterexamples

The following candidates require investigation.

- Reflexive behavior.
- Instinctive behavior.
- Neural computation.
- Reinforcement learning.
- Evolutionary optimization.
- Intuition.
- Pattern recognition.

---

## Analysis

Reflexes do not appear to perform reasoning.

They execute predetermined responses to stimuli.

Accordingly, reflexive behavior is not presently regarded as a counterexample.

---

Instinctive behavior similarly appears to consist primarily of predetermined behavioral patterns rather than explicit reasoning.

Whether instinct can participate in reasoning remains an open question.

---

Neural computation performs information processing.

Whether information processing alone constitutes reasoning remains unresolved.

Accordingly, neural computation does not presently establish that reasoning can occur without representations.

---

Reinforcement learning and evolutionary optimization improve behavior through iterative adaptation.

However, improvement alone does not imply reasoning.

Whether these processes manipulate representations in the sense required by Project FAR remains under investigation.

---

Pattern recognition identifies regularities within data.

Recognition itself does not necessarily constitute reasoning.

Additional investigation is required.

---

Intuition presents the strongest candidate.

Human beings often report arriving at conclusions without conscious representations or explicit reasoning.

However, unconscious reasoning may still employ representations that are not introspectively accessible.

The absence of conscious representations therefore does not establish the absence of representations.

---

## Observations

Every candidate counterexample identified thus far can be interpreted in one of two ways:

- it is not reasoning; or
- it may involve representations that are implicit rather than explicit.

No candidate has yet demonstrated reasoning entirely independent of representation.

---

## Current Assessment

No convincing counterexample has presently been identified.

Current evidence therefore supports the working hypothesis that every reasoning process within the intended scope of Project FAR is representational.

This conclusion remains provisional pending further investigation.

---

## Open Questions

- Must representations always be explicit?
- Can unconscious representations satisfy the requirements of Project FAR?
- Does Project FAR model reasoning itself or only explicit representations of reasoning?
- Should implicit representations fall within the scope of the framework?

---

# Investigation 4 — Does Project FAR Model Reasoning or Representations of Reasoning?

## Status

In Progress

---

## Research Question

What is the primary object of study within Project FAR?

Does Project FAR model reasoning itself, or does it model explicit representations of reasoning?

---

## Motivation

The answer to this question determines the scope of Project FAR.

It also determines the meaning of every primitive concept within the framework.

---

## Candidate Hypothesis A

Project FAR models reasoning itself.

### Assessment

This approach would require Project FAR to describe internal cognitive processes regardless of whether they can be explicitly represented.

Such processes may not be directly observable.

Consequently, they may not admit objective analysis.

This approach therefore appears incompatible with the objective of constructing a formal framework.

Status:

Problematic.

---

## Candidate Hypothesis B

Project FAR models explicit representations of reasoning.

### Assessment

Under this approach, Project FAR makes no claims regarding hidden cognitive processes.

Instead, it studies reasoning only insofar as it has been explicitly represented.

Such representations may be produced by:

- humans;
- artificial intelligence systems;
- mathematical proofs;
- scientific arguments;
- legal arguments;
- philosophical arguments; or
- any other source.

Because the representations are explicit, they may be objectively analyzed, compared, verified, and evaluated.

Status:

Promising.

---

## Analysis

Reasoning itself may be inaccessible.

Different cognitive systems may perform reasoning through entirely different internal mechanisms.

However, once reasoning is expressed through explicit representations, those representations become objectively available for formal investigation.

Project FAR therefore need not explain how reasoning occurs internally.

Instead, it provides a universal framework for representing and analyzing reasoning once it has been made explicit.

This distinction separates Project FAR from theories of cognition, psychology, neuroscience, and artificial intelligence.

---

## Implications

If Candidate Hypothesis B is adopted:

- Project FAR is not a theory of cognition.
- Project FAR is not a theory of intelligence.
- Project FAR does not model internal mental states.
- Project FAR models explicit representational artifacts produced through reasoning.

Accordingly, the scope of Project FAR becomes objectively identifiable.

---

## Current Assessment

Current evidence strongly favors Candidate Hypothesis B.

Project FAR appears to be a framework for representing, analyzing, and evaluating explicit representations of reasoning rather than reasoning considered independently of representation.

---

## Consequences for the Primitive Architecture

If this conclusion is adopted, Representation becomes the natural entry point of the primitive architecture.

Every reasoning process within the scope of Project FAR must first admit an explicit representation before formal analysis can begin.

---

## Open Questions

- What constitutes an explicit representation?
- When is a reasoning process sufficiently represented to fall within the scope of Project FAR?
- Can different representations encode the same reasoning process?
- How should equivalence between representations be defined?

---

## Current Conclusion

Current investigations indicate that Project FAR is most naturally interpreted as a theory of explicit representations of reasoning rather than a theory of cognition or reasoning itself.

This conclusion remains provisional pending further investigation.

---

# Investigation 5 — What Constitutes an Explicit Representation?

## Status

In Progress

---

## Research Question

What conditions must be satisfied for a representation to be considered explicit within Project FAR?

---

## Motivation

The current investigations suggest that Project FAR studies explicit representations of reasoning rather than reasoning itself.

Accordingly, the concept of explicit representation determines the boundary of the framework.

Without a precise definition, the scope of Project FAR remains ambiguous.

---

## Candidate Definition A

A representation is explicit if it can be observed.

### Assessment

Insufficient.

Many observable objects are not representations.

Visibility alone does not establish representational status.

Rejected.

---

## Candidate Definition B

A representation is explicit if it is externally recorded.

### Assessment

Too restrictive.

A person may consciously state an argument verbally without producing a permanent record.

The reasoning is still explicit.

Rejected.

---

## Candidate Definition C

A representation is explicit if its content can be identified independently of the internal cognitive state that produced it.

### Assessment

Promising.

Under this definition, an explicit representation possesses objective identity.

Different investigators can examine the same representation without requiring access to the thinker's internal mental processes.

Status:

Under Investigation.

---

## Candidate Definition D

A representation is explicit if it is sufficiently specified to permit formal analysis.

### Assessment

Promising.

This definition aligns naturally with the objectives of Project FAR.

A representation enters the scope of the framework when enough information has been made explicit to permit objective analysis.

Further investigation is required.

Status:

Under Investigation.

---

## Observations

Explicitness appears to concern accessibility rather than physical form.

An explicit representation may exist as:

- written text;
- spoken language;
- symbolic notation;
- diagrams;
- formal proofs;
- structured data;
- computational representations.

The physical medium appears incidental.

What matters is that the representation can be objectively identified and analyzed.

---

## Open Questions

- What minimum information must be explicit?
- Can incomplete representations fall within the scope of Project FAR?
- Can two different explicit representations encode the same reasoning process?
- Does explicitness admit degrees?
- Is explicitness binary or continuous?

---

## Current Assessment

Current evidence suggests that explicitness is a property of objective accessibility rather than physical realization.

A complete definition has not yet been established.

---

## Current Conclusion

The concept of explicit representation remains under investigation.

Its eventual definition will determine the formal boundary of Project FAR.

# Investigation 8 — Necessary and Sufficient Conditions for Membership in the Scope of Project FAR

## Status

In Progress

---

## Research Question

What conditions are jointly necessary and sufficient for a process to fall within the scope of Project FAR?

---

## Motivation

Previous investigations established that Project FAR studies explicit representations of reasoning rather than cognition itself.

The remaining task is to determine the precise conditions under which a process belongs within the framework.

---

## Candidate Necessary Conditions

### Condition 1 — Reasoning

The process must constitute reasoning.

Processes consisting solely of physical causation, reflexive behavior, or arbitrary computation do not satisfy this condition.

Status:

Under Investigation.

---

### Condition 2 — Explicit Representation

The reasoning must admit an explicit representation.

The representation must be objectively identifiable independently of the internal cognitive state that produced it.

Status:

Provisionally Accepted.

---

### Condition 3 — Formal Admissibility

The explicit representation must admit formal analysis using the primitive architecture of Project FAR.

Specifically, the representation must admit identification of:

- an Investigation;
- one or more Representations;
- a Representational Structure;
- one or more Interpretations; and
- a Reasoning Calculus.

Status:

Provisionally Accepted.

---

## Sufficiency Analysis

Suppose a process satisfies all three conditions.

1. It is reasoning.
2. It possesses an explicit representation.
3. That representation admits formal analysis through the primitive architecture.

Nothing further appears necessary for Project FAR to represent, analyze, and evaluate the reasoning process.

Accordingly, these conditions constitute a promising candidate for jointly sufficient membership.

---

## Borderline Cases

The following cases require evaluation.

### Mental reasoning lacking external expression

Fails Condition 2.

Outside the scope of Project FAR.

---

### Mathematical proof

Satisfies all three conditions.

Within scope.

---

### Legal argument

Satisfies all three conditions.

Within scope.

---

### Scientific explanation

Satisfies all three conditions.

Within scope.

---

### Computer algorithm performing arithmetic

Satisfies Condition 2.

Whether it satisfies Condition 1 depends upon whether the computation constitutes reasoning rather than mere computation.

Further investigation required.

---

### Reflexive behavior

Fails Condition 1.

Outside the scope.

---

### Random process

Fails Condition 1.

Outside the scope.

---

## Emerging Scope Criterion

Current investigations suggest that Project FAR is concerned with:

> Every reasoning process that admits an explicit representation capable of formal analysis under the primitive architecture.

This statement remains provisional.

---

## Remaining Open Question

One fundamental question remains unresolved.

What precisely distinguishes reasoning from general computation?

Until this question is resolved, the scope of Project FAR cannot be regarded as complete.

---

## Current Assessment

Current investigations indicate that the boundary of Project FAR is determined structurally rather than by discipline, implementation, or physical realization.

The principal remaining uncertainty concerns the definition of reasoning itself.
