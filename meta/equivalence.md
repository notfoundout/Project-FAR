# Equivalence

## Purpose

This document defines equivalence within the meta-theory of Project FAR.

Equivalence specifies when two objects should be regarded as identical for the purposes of formal analysis despite possible differences in representation.

It provides the theoretical basis for comparison, substitution, reduction, and normalization throughout Project FAR.

---

# Definition

Two objects are **equivalent** if every distinction between them is irrelevant with respect to the property under investigation.

Accordingly, equivalence is always defined relative to an explicitly identified criterion.

---

# Relative Nature of Equivalence

Equivalence is not absolute.

Every statement of equivalence shall identify:

- the objects being compared;
- the criterion of comparison; and
- the context in which equivalence is asserted.

Objects may therefore be equivalent with respect to one criterion while remaining distinct with respect to another.

---

# Identity and Equivalence

Identity and equivalence are distinct concepts.

Identity requires complete correspondence.

Equivalent objects need not be identical.

Different objects may satisfy the same equivalence criterion.

Accordingly:

- Identity implies equivalence.
- Equivalence does not imply identity.

---

# Equivalence Criteria

Project FAR recognizes that multiple equivalence relations may exist.

Examples include:

- structural equivalence;
- semantic equivalence;
- procedural equivalence;
- operational equivalence.

Additional equivalence relations may be introduced where formally justified.

---

# Structural Equivalence

Two objects are structurally equivalent if they possess identical structural organization under the chosen representation.

Structural equivalence concerns organization rather than meaning.

---

# Semantic Equivalence

Two objects are semantically equivalent if they express the same meaning despite differences in representation.

Semantic equivalence concerns interpretation rather than structure.

---

# Operational Equivalence

Two objects are operationally equivalent if every admissible operation within Project FAR produces equivalent results.

Operational equivalence concerns behavior rather than representation.

---

# Equivalence Classes

Every equivalence relation partitions a collection of objects into equivalence classes.

Members of the same equivalence class are interchangeable with respect to the defining criterion.

---

# Relationship to Derivability

Derivability and equivalence are distinct.

Derivability concerns whether one concept is determined by others.

Equivalence concerns whether two objects should be regarded as interchangeable under a specified criterion.

Neither concept implies the other.

---

# Relationship to FARO

Many FARO operations depend upon equivalence.

Examples include:

- comparison;
- redundancy detection;
- normalization;
- substitution;
- simplification.

Accordingly, equivalence serves as a foundational concept for the operational framework.
