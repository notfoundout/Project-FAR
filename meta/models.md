# Models

## Purpose

This document defines the concept of a model within the meta-theory of Project FAR.

Models provide formal interpretations of the concepts defined by the framework and constitute the primary objects used to investigate representation, derivability, equivalence, and independence.

---

# Definition

A **model** is a complete interpretation of the concepts required to instantiate a reasoning process within the scope of Project FAR.

A model assigns explicit values to every concept required for the investigation being performed.

---

# Purpose of Models

Models serve four principal purposes.

- Represent reasoning processes.
- Evaluate theoretical claims.
- Construct counterexamples.
- Investigate derivability and independence.

Accordingly, models are analytical objects rather than reasoning processes themselves.

---

# Completeness

A model is complete if every concept required by the current investigation has been explicitly specified.

Incomplete models may be useful during exploratory research but cannot establish formal theoretical results.

---

# Equality of Models

Two models are equal if every corresponding component possesses identical values.

They are distinct if at least one corresponding component differs.

---

# Model Variation

A model variation is a pair or collection of models intentionally constructed to differ in one or more specified components while remaining identical in all others.

Model variation permits controlled investigation of theoretical dependence.

---

# Model Separation

Model separation is a particular form of model variation.

Two models are model-separated with respect to concept C if:

- every component other than C is identical; and
- C differs between the models.

Model separation is currently investigated as a possible method for establishing non-derivability.

The formal justification of this method remains part of the active research program.

---

# Canonical Models

A canonical model is a model constructed solely for theoretical investigation.

Canonical models need not correspond to actual reasoning processes.

Their purpose is to investigate the logical properties of the framework.

---

# Empirical Models

An empirical model is constructed from an actual reasoning process.

Empirical models are used to demonstrate, evaluate, and validate Project FAR against real-world reasoning.

---

# Model Classes

Project FAR presently recognizes two classes of models.

## Canonical Models

Constructed for theoretical investigation.

Primary uses include:

- theorem investigation;
- counterexample construction;
- independence analysis.

---

## Empirical Models

Constructed from observed reasoning.

Primary uses include:

- validation;
- demonstration;
- application;
- comparison.

---

# Relationship to the Theory

Models instantiate the concepts defined by the object theory.

They do not modify the theory.

Instead, they provide concrete objects upon which theoretical investigations are performed.

---

# Current Research

The following questions remain under investigation.

- What minimum information is required for model completeness?
- Can different models represent equivalent reasoning?
- How should model equivalence be formally defined?
- Can model separation universally establish non-derivability?
