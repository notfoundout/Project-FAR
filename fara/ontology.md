# Ontology

## Introduction

An ontology specifies the kinds of things that exist within a reasoning framework.

The objective of FARA is to identify the smallest ontology capable of representing every structured, explicit, auditable reasoning process within its stated scope.

Every ontological category is therefore treated as provisional and remains subject to elimination through successful reduction.

---

# Design Principle

The ontology should contain no unnecessary categories.

Whenever possible, apparently distinct objects should be represented using existing ontological categories rather than introducing new primitives.

---

# Current Ontology

The current ontology consists of four primitive categories.

1. Representational Structure
2. Interpretation
3. Investigation Question
4. Reasoning Calculus

Everything else is presently treated as derived.

---

# Representations

Representations are distinguishable objects within a representational structure.

A representation may describe:

- an entity,
- a relation,
- a hypothesis,
- an observation,
- a definition,
- a rule,
- a proof,
- a model,
- or any other explicitly representable object.

FARA does not distinguish among these at the ontological level.

They differ only by interpretation.

---

# Interpretation

Interpretation assigns semantic content to representations.

Identical representations may possess different meanings under different interpretations.

Interpretation therefore cannot presently be reduced to representational structure alone.

---

# Investigation

Every reasoning process exists relative to an investigation.

The investigation determines:

- what counts as progress,
- what counts as relevance,
- what constitutes a successful resolution.

Without an investigation, reasoning has no explicit objective.

---

# Reasoning Calculus

The reasoning calculus specifies how representations are evaluated.

Different calculi may produce different admissibility structures from identical representations.

Examples include:

- deductive inference,
- probabilistic inference,
- optimization,
- legal reasoning,
- scientific explanation.

The ontology therefore treats the reasoning calculus as independent of representation.

---

# Derived Objects

The following are currently regarded as derived.

## Reasoning State

The complete explicit configuration of representations associated with an investigation.

---

## Possibility Space

The collection of candidate resolutions generated by the representational structure, interpretation, and investigation.

---

## Ω

The admissibility structure induced by applying the reasoning calculus to the possibility space.

---

## Resolution

The justified reduction of the investigation's possibility space.

---

# Minimality

The ontology should remain as small as possible.

Every additional ontological category introduces explanatory cost.

Accordingly, new primitives should be introduced only when they cannot be represented using the existing ontology without loss of expressive power.

---

# Open Problems

The ontology remains incomplete.

Future work includes:

- independence proofs,
- formal semantics,
- representation theorems,
- attempts to eliminate surviving primitives,
- external validation against competing foundational frameworks.

No primitive should be regarded as permanent until its independence has been formally established.
