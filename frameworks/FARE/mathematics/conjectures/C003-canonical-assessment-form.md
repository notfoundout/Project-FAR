# FARE Mathematical Conjecture

## Identifier

FARE-C003

---

# Title

Canonical Assessment Form

---

## Status

Conjecture

---

# Question

Does every finite assessment graph admit a canonical form under assessment-preserving equivalence?

---

# Motivation

Equivalent assessment graphs may differ in representation while encoding the same evaluative structure.

A canonical form would provide a unique representative for every equivalence class, enabling structural comparison independent of representation.

---

# Informal Statement

Every finite assessment graph can be transformed into a unique canonical representative without changing its evaluative meaning.

---

# Required Definitions

This conjecture requires canonical definitions for:

- assessment graph equivalence;
- canonical form;
- canonical transformation;
- assessment-preserving transformation;
- graph normalization.

---

# Formal Statement

Pending definition.

---

# Initial Hypothesis

Canonical forms are likely to exist only after an appropriate notion of graph equivalence has been defined.

The uniqueness of the canonical form depends upon that equivalence relation.

---

# Counterexample Search

Potential counterexample:

Two assessment graphs contain identical assessments and dependency relationships but differ only in node identifiers or ordering.

If these graphs cannot be transformed into the same canonical representation, the proposed canonicalization procedure is incomplete.

Conversely, if two graphs with different evaluative meanings normalize to the same representation, the canonicalization procedure is unsound.

---

# Research Questions

- What properties define assessment graph equivalence?
- Which graph properties must remain invariant?
- Which properties may change during normalization?
- Does every equivalence class possess a unique canonical representative?

---

# Possible Results

## Result 1

Every finite assessment graph possesses a unique canonical form.

---

## Result 2

Canonical forms exist only for restricted classes of assessment graphs.

---

## Result 3

No universal canonical form exists.

Instead, multiple canonicalization procedures are required for different classes of assessment graphs.

---

# Applications

A canonical form would support:

- graph comparison;
- duplicate detection;
- repository normalization;
- proof optimization;
- efficient storage;
- deterministic serialization.

---

# Notes

This conjecture should remain open until both graph equivalence and assessment-preserving transformations have been formally defined.

If proven, this result would provide a foundation for many future algorithms operating on assessment graphs.
