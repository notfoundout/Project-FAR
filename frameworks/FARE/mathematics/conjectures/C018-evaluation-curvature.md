# FARE Mathematical Conjecture

## Identifier

FARE-C018

---

# Title

Evaluation Curvature

---

## Status

Conjecture

---

# Question

Does the space of evaluations possess a well-defined notion of curvature induced by its transformation structure?

---

# Motivation

If evaluation distance and geodesics exist, then the geometry of evaluation spaces may possess local and global curvature.

Curvature would quantify how the structure of evaluations influences the behavior of optimal transformation paths.

Understanding curvature could reveal why certain reasoning problems are inherently more difficult to optimize or normalize than others.

---

# Informal Statement

The local structure of an evaluation space determines how shortest evaluation-preserving transformation paths behave.

---

# Required Definitions

This conjecture requires canonical definitions for:

- evaluation space;
- evaluation distance;
- evaluation geodesic;
- local neighborhood;
- evaluation curvature;
- geodesic deviation.

---

# Formal Statement

Pending definition.

---

# Initial Hypothesis

Evaluation curvature is unlikely to correspond directly to any existing notion of geometric curvature.

Instead, it may emerge from the branching structure and interaction of evaluation-preserving transformations.

Different regions of an evaluation space may possess different curvature characteristics.

---

# Counterexample Search

Construct evaluation spaces containing:

- multiple equivalent shortest transformation paths;
- highly constrained normalization paths;
- dense transformation neighborhoods;
- sparse transformation neighborhoods.

Determine whether these structures exhibit distinguishable geometric behavior.

---

# Research Questions

- Can curvature be defined intrinsically?
- Does curvature remain invariant under evaluation equivalence?
- Can regions of positive and negative curvature exist?
- Does curvature influence optimization difficulty?
- Does curvature constrain geodesic uniqueness?

# Notes

This conjecture does not assume any existing geometric definition of curvature.

Instead, it investigates whether the transformation structure induced by FARE naturally gives rise to a meaningful notion of curvature.

If such a notion exists, it should be defined entirely in terms of FARE's own formal objects and transformation rules.
