# FARE Mathematical Conjecture

## Identifier

FARE-C013

---

# Title

Evaluation Dimension

---

## Status

Conjecture

---

# Question

Can every finite evaluation be assigned a well-defined evaluation dimension?

---

# Motivation

Many mathematical structures possess intrinsic measures of complexity or dimensionality.

If evaluations possess an analogous notion of dimension, then evaluation complexity may be characterized independently of implementation.

---

# Informal Statement

Every finite evaluation possesses one or more measurable dimensions determined by its structural properties.

---

# Required Definitions

This conjecture requires canonical definitions for:

- evaluation dimension;
- structural dimension;
- evaluative complexity;
- dimension-preserving transformation;
- dimensional equivalence.

---

# Formal Statement

Pending definition.

---

# Initial Hypothesis

Evaluation dimension is unlikely to be represented by a single numerical quantity.

Multiple independent dimensions may exist.

Examples may include:

- dependency dimension;
- support dimension;
- conflict dimension;
- informational dimension.

---

# Counterexample Search

Construct evaluations with:

- identical numbers of assessments;
- different dependency topologies;
- different support densities;
- different conflict structures.

Determine whether identical evaluation dimensions adequately distinguish these structures.

---

# Research Questions

- What properties contribute to evaluation dimension?
- Is dimension invariant under evaluation equivalence?
- Can dimension increase under composition?
- Can dimension decrease through normalization?
- Are multiple dimensions necessary?

---

# Possible Results

## Result 1

Every finite evaluation possesses a unique evaluation dimension.

---

## Result 2

Evaluation requires a multidimensional invariant.

---

## Result 3

No useful notion of evaluation dimension exists.

---

## Result 4

Different classes of evaluations require different dimensional measures.

# Notes

This conjecture introduces the possibility that evaluations possess intrinsic structural invariants analogous to dimension in other areas of mathematics.

The objective is not to impose an existing mathematical notion of dimension, but to determine whether a meaningful and useful concept of dimension naturally emerges from the formal structures defined by FARE.
