# FARE Mathematical Conjecture

## Identifier

FARE-C007

---

# Title

Evaluation Equivalence

---

## Status

Conjecture

---

# Question

Under what conditions do two evaluations represent the same evaluative result?

---

# Motivation

Different evaluation procedures may produce identical conclusions through different intermediate reasoning.

If these evaluations are equivalent, they should be treated as representing the same evaluative outcome.

Establishing evaluation equivalence is essential for comparison, optimization, and normalization.

---

# Informal Statement

Two evaluations are equivalent if they preserve the same evaluative meaning under an accepted equivalence relation.

---

# Required Definitions

This conjecture requires canonical definitions for:

- evaluation equivalence;
- evaluative meaning;
- equivalent evaluation;
- evaluation transformation;
- semantic preservation.

---

# Formal Statement

Pending definition.

---

# Initial Hypothesis

Evaluation equivalence depends upon the selected equivalence relation.

Different notions of equivalence may exist for different investigations.

---

# Counterexample Search

Construct evaluations that:

- reach identical conclusions through different reasoning paths;
- differ only in assessment ordering;
- differ only by redundant supporting assessments;
- differ only by graph representation.

Determine whether each pair should be considered equivalent.

---

# Research Questions

- Which properties must remain invariant?
- Which structural differences are irrelevant?
- Can equivalent evaluations always be transformed into one another?
- Is there a unique canonical representative for each equivalence class?

---

# Possible Results

## Result 1

A unique evaluation equivalence relation exists.

---

## Result 2

Multiple useful equivalence relations exist for different purposes.

---

## Result 3

No single equivalence relation adequately captures every notion of evaluative sameness.

# Notes

This conjecture concerns formal equivalence within FARE.

It does not imply that equivalent evaluations arise from identical reasoning processes.

Rather, it investigates when different evaluation structures should be regarded as representing the same evaluative result.
