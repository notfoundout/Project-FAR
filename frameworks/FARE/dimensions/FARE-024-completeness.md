# FARE Discovery Investigation

## Identifier

FARE-024

---

# Title

Completeness

---

## Status

In Progress

---

# Purpose

Determine the necessary and sufficient conditions under which an assessment is complete.

The objective is to establish completeness as a formal assessment property within the Formal Architecture of Reasoning Evaluation.

---

# Motivation

Previous investigations established:

- validity;
- justification;
- admissibility.

An assessment may satisfy all three while nevertheless omitting relevant evaluative considerations.

This investigation examines that distinction.

---

# Central Question

What makes an assessment complete?

---

# Candidate 1

Large Amount of Information.

Question

Does more information produce greater completeness?

Evaluation

Additional information may be irrelevant.

Quantity alone is insufficient.

Result

Rejected.

---

# Candidate 2

Exhaustiveness.

Question

Must every possible consideration be included?

Evaluation

Many possible considerations possess no evaluative relevance.

Exhaustiveness exceeds what is necessary.

Result

Rejected.

---

# Candidate 3

Relevant Coverage.

Question

Is an assessment complete when it considers every evaluatively relevant element required by its evaluation objective?

Evaluation

Current evidence supports this hypothesis.

Completeness depends upon relevance rather than quantity.

Result

Supported.

---

# Pattern Analysis

Every investigated complete assessment possesses:

- an evaluation objective;
- evaluatively relevant elements;
- explicit consideration of those elements.

Omitting relevant elements reduces completeness.

Including irrelevant elements does not increase completeness.

---

# Emerging Hypothesis

Completeness is the property of an assessment that considers every evaluatively relevant element required by its evaluation objective.

---

# Observation

Completeness depends upon relevance.

As investigations identify additional relevant elements, previously complete assessments may become incomplete.

Completeness is therefore relative to available evaluative knowledge.

---

# Consequences

If accepted:

Project FAR distinguishes:

- validity;
- justification;
- admissibility;
- completeness.

Each evaluates a different aspect of an assessment.

---

---

# Remaining Questions

Future investigations should determine:

- whether completeness admits degrees;
- whether completeness is monotonic;
- whether completeness depends upon available information;
- whether completeness can be formally demonstrated.

---

# Current Status

The investigation remains active.

Current evidence supports treating completeness as the coverage of all evaluatively relevant considerations required by an evaluation objective.
