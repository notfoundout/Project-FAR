# FI-000 — What Is the Correct Foundational Research Question?

## Status

Active Investigation

## Purpose

Determine the correct research question for discovering the foundations of explicit reasoning.

This investigation precedes all grounding investigations.

It does not attempt to answer questions about reasoning itself until the project has justified that it is asking the correct question.

## Motivation

A research program can fail before its first proof if it begins from an incorrect or incomplete question.

Therefore the formulation of the research question is itself a foundational problem.

## Primary Question

What question must be answered first in order to discover the foundations of explicit reasoning?

## Candidate Questions

The following are research candidates only.

- What is reasoning?
- What is explicit reasoning?
- What is the weakest possible structure under which explicit reasoning can exist?
- What properties are invariant across all explicit reasoning?
- What conditions are necessary for explicit reasoning?
- Does explicit reasoning possess a unique canonical foundation?

No candidate is currently privileged.

## Evaluation Criteria

The preferred research question should:

- avoid circularity;
- avoid assuming the desired framework;
- admit objective investigation;
- minimize assumptions;
- maximize explanatory power;
- expose rather than conceal hidden assumptions;
- naturally generate downstream investigations.

## Initial Observation

The question "What is the weakest possible structure under which explicit reasoning can exist?" currently appears promising because it does not explicitly assume FAR, FARA, FARO, representations, states, transitions, or other existing framework concepts.

However, this observation is provisional and does not establish that it is the correct foundational question.

## Phase 1 — Candidate Question Analysis

### Candidate A — What is reasoning?

#### Strength

This question targets the broadest phenomenon.

#### Defect

It is too broad for the current project because Project FAR is concerned with explicit, inspectable, auditable reasoning rather than every possible cognitive or biological process that may be called reasoning.

#### Risk

Starting here may force the project to solve consciousness, cognition, intelligence, or psychology before grounding explicit reasoning.

#### Current Disposition

Rejected as the first foundational question.

It remains relevant as a broader background question.

### Candidate B — What is explicit reasoning?

#### Strength

This question matches the intended scope more closely than Candidate A.

#### Defect

It still invites premature definition.

If the project defines explicit reasoning before discovering what makes explicit reasoning possible, the definition may smuggle in assumed framework components.

#### Risk

The answer may become stipulative rather than discovered.

#### Current Disposition

Deferred.

This question should likely follow the first structural grounding question rather than precede it.

### Candidate C — What is the weakest possible structure under which explicit reasoning can exist?

#### Strength

This question asks for minimal enabling conditions rather than a preferred framework.

It does not explicitly assume FAR, FARA, FARO, representation, state, transition, candidate, admissibility, or resolution.

It naturally generates necessity tests, reduction tests, primitive searches, and minimal-model construction.

#### Defect

It assumes that explicit reasoning can be analyzed structurally.

It also assumes that the phrase "weakest possible structure" is meaningful and can be compared across alternatives.

#### Risk

If "structure" is interpreted too narrowly, the investigation may bias itself toward architectural answers.

#### Current Disposition

Strong candidate.

Not yet accepted.

### Candidate D — What properties are invariant across all explicit reasoning?

#### Strength

This question searches for stable features rather than preferred components.

It may avoid premature commitment to primitives.

#### Defect

It requires a prior domain: all explicit reasoning.

Without an independent account of what counts as explicit reasoning, invariant search may become circular.

#### Current Disposition

Deferred.

Likely useful after a provisional domain is established.

### Candidate E — What conditions are necessary for explicit reasoning?

#### Strength

This question directly targets necessities.

#### Defect

It may begin too close to the desired output.

If the project starts by looking for necessities, it may overlook whether necessity is the right explanatory form.

#### Current Disposition

Deferred.

This question becomes appropriate after the project establishes the correct object and scope of inquiry.

### Candidate F — Does explicit reasoning possess a unique canonical foundation?

#### Strength

This question tests whether Project FAR is searching for the foundation or a space of possible foundations.

#### Defect

It is downstream of discovering candidate foundations.

Without candidates, uniqueness cannot be evaluated.

#### Current Disposition

Deferred.

It becomes important after multiple candidate structures are available for comparison.

## Phase 1 Interim Result

The current best candidate is:

> What is the weakest possible structure under which explicit reasoning can exist?

This is not accepted as canonical yet.

It is currently preferred because it has the lowest apparent commitment to the existing framework and generates concrete downstream tests.

## Hidden Assumptions Identified

The preferred candidate currently depends on at least the following assumptions:

- explicit reasoning is a legitimate object of inquiry;
- explicit reasoning can be analyzed structurally;
- enabling conditions can be compared by strength or weakness;
- minimality is relevant to foundational discovery;
- a framework should be derived only after its enabling conditions are understood.

These assumptions remain unproven.

## Methodology Audit

### Did the methodology expose hidden assumptions?

Yes.

The methodology forced explicit identification of assumptions behind the preferred question.

### Did it prevent circular reasoning?

Partially.

It prevented immediate acceptance of a candidate question but has not yet provided a complete circularity test for foundational questions.

### Did it prevent premature conclusions?

Yes.

The candidate question remains preferred but not canonical.

### Were any required tests inapplicable or insufficient?

The current methodology does not yet specify a complete test for comparing research questions themselves.

### Did a new methodological issue emerge?

Yes.

The project needs a way to evaluate research questions independently from candidate theoretical claims.

### Does the doctrine require revision?

Not yet.

The issue can be handled inside FI-000 for now.

If repeated investigations expose the same gap, the methodology should be revised.

## Deliverable

The outcome of FI-000 shall be one of:

- a single canonical foundational research question;
- a justified family of foundational questions with dependency order; or
- a demonstration that no unique foundational question exists.

No other investigation may claim to begin from first principles until FI-000 has either been completed or its dependency status explicitly justified.
