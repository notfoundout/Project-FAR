# Theorem T1 — Limits of Root-Theoretical Construction

## Status

Provisionally Proven

---

## Statement

The Root Theory cannot explicitly construct a non-primitive result using only the concepts admitted by the Root Theory.

Accordingly,

the first explicit construction belongs to the Meta-Theory.

---

## Purpose

This theorem establishes the constructive boundary between the Root Theory and the Meta-Theory.

It does not determine what the first Meta-Theoretical construction is.

Rather,

it determines where explicit construction must begin.

---

## Accepted Dependencies

The following dependencies are assumed accepted.

- Accepted Methodology
- Accepted Root Theory

No additional dependencies are permitted.

---

## Proof

Assume the Root Theory explicitly constructs a non-primitive result.

An explicit construction must specify the result being constructed.

If specification requires only concepts already admitted by the Root Theory,

then the Root Theory contains concepts beyond its primitive basis.

This contradicts the accepted purpose of the Root Theory,

which is to establish the primitive basis rather than introduce derived concepts.

If specification requires concepts not admitted by the Root Theory,

then the construction necessarily employs concepts belonging to a higher layer.

Accordingly,

the explicit construction cannot be completed entirely within the Root Theory.

Therefore,

the first explicit construction belongs to the Meta-Theory.

---

---

- an explicit non-primitive construction using only Root-Theoretical concepts;
- a Root Theory that introduces derived concepts without exceeding its accepted scope.

No successful counterexample has presently been demonstrated.

---

---

## Consequences

If this theorem survives future falsification,

then:

- the Root Theory terminates upon establishing its primitive basis;
- explicit construction begins within the Meta-Theory;
- every derived formal object belongs to the Meta-Theory or a higher layer.

---

# Theorem T3 — Exclusivity of the Primitive Basis

## Status

Under Proof

---

## Statement

Accordingly,

every subsequently admitted concept is either:

- derived; or
- rejected.

---

## Purpose

This theorem establishes that primitive selection terminates.

It prevents indefinite expansion of the primitive basis.

---

## Accepted Dependencies

The following dependencies are assumed accepted.

- Accepted Methodology
- Accepted Root Theory
- Theorem T1 — Limits of Root-Theoretical Construction
- Theorem T2 — Completeness of the Root Theory

No additional dependencies are permitted.

---

## Proof

Assume a new primitive is admitted after the canonical primitive basis has already been established.

If the new primitive is indispensable,

then the previously accepted primitive basis was incomplete.

This contradicts the assumption that the primitive basis was canonical.

If the new primitive is not indispensable,

its admission violates the principles of minimality and reduction.

Accordingly,

no additional primitive may be admitted after completion of the Root Theory.

Therefore,

every subsequently admitted concept is necessarily derived or rejected.

# Theorem T5 — Termination of Primitive Evaluation

## Status

Under Proof

---

## Statement

Primitive evaluation terminates if and only if a canonical primitive basis has been established.

Until then,

the Root Theory remains incomplete.

---

## Purpose

This theorem establishes the objective termination criterion of primitive evaluation.

It prevents arbitrary acceptance or indefinite continuation of primitive evaluation.

---

## Accepted Dependencies

The following dependencies are assumed accepted.

- Accepted Methodology
- Accepted Root Theory
- Theorem T2 — Completeness of the Root Theory
- Canonical Primitive Evaluation Framework

No additional dependencies are permitted.

---

## Proof

Suppose primitive evaluation terminates before a canonical primitive basis has been established.

Then no objective justification exists for terminating evaluation.

Accordingly,

termination is arbitrary.

This contradicts the accepted methodology.

Now suppose a canonical primitive basis has been established.

Further primitive evaluation can produce only one of three outcomes.

- A previously accepted primitive is rejected.
- A new indispensable primitive is identified.
- No change occurs.

If the first or second outcome occurs,

the previously accepted basis was not canonical.

If the third outcome occurs,

primitive evaluation has reached a fixed point.

Accordingly,

primitive evaluation terminates if and only if the canonical primitive basis has been established.
