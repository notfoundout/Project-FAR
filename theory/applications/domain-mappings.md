# Domain Mappings

## Purpose

This document records initial FAR mappings across common reasoning domains.

## Logical Proof

A proof maps to `<I, Rep, S, Int, C, T>` by treating the goal as `I`, formulas as `Rep`, proof dependencies as `S`, formula meanings as `Int`, inference rules as `C`, and proof steps as `T`.

## Probabilistic Update

A probability update maps to `<I, Rep, S, Int, C, T>` by treating the update objective as `I`, hypotheses and evidence as `Rep`, conditional dependency as `S`, probability assignments as `Int`, update rules as `C`, and update steps as `T`.

## Empirical Inquiry

An inquiry maps to `<I, Rep, S, Int, C, T>` by treating the research question as `I`, observations and hypotheses as `Rep`, evidential relations as `S`, measurement meaning as `Int`, method rules as `C`, and the inquiry sequence as `T`.

## Legal Argument

A legal argument maps to `<I, Rep, S, Int, C, T>` by treating the dispute as `I`, facts and authorities as `Rep`, dependency and authority relations as `S`, legal meanings as `Int`, jurisdictional standards as `C`, and argument steps as `T`.

## Everyday Argument

An everyday argument maps to `<I, Rep, S, Int, C, T>` by treating the question as `I`, claims and objections as `Rep`, support and conflict relations as `S`, ordinary-language meanings as `Int`, reasoning standards as `C`, and conversational moves as `T`.

## Result

These mappings support the Representation Theorem by showing that different reasoning systems instantiate the same FAR tuple without requiring the same reasoning calculus.
