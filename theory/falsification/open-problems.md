# Primitive Sufficiency Open Problems

Status: Provisional v0.3.0 falsification agenda.

These cases are future hard tests for the primitive-sufficiency framework. They are not v0.2.0 result rewrites.

## Modal logic

- Why it matters: Modal operators test whether necessity and possibility require primitive status or can be treated as interpreted structures and calculus rules.
- What would support FAR: A fixture represents modal accessibility, worlds, and modal inference as structures, interpretations, and calculus policies.
- What would challenge FAR: A reproducible modal case requires an irreducible primitive beyond representation, structure, interpretation, and calculus.

## Temporal logic

- Why it matters: Temporal ordering tests whether time-indexed reasoning is reducible to representational structure and transition policy.
- What would support FAR: A fixture represents temporal states, ordering relations, and temporal inference without adding a time primitive.
- What would challenge FAR: Temporal validity requires an indispensable primitive not expressible as structure, interpretation, or calculus.

## Deontic logic

- Why it matters: Obligation and permission test normativity as a potential primitive-pressure case.
- What would support FAR: A fixture treats duties, permissions, and violations as interpreted representations governed by deontic calculus rules.
- What would challenge FAR: Normative force cannot be represented without a new primitive distinct from interpretation and calculus.

## Intuitionistic logic

- Why it matters: Constructive proof conditions test whether proof evidence requires more than current primitive roles.
- What would support FAR: A fixture represents constructions and proof obligations as structures and calculus rules.
- What would challenge FAR: Constructive validity requires a primitive proof-evidence category irreducible to existing primitives.

## Fuzzy logic

- Why it matters: Graded truth tests whether degrees require primitive expansion or only interpreted values and calculus policies.
- What would support FAR: A fixture represents truth degrees and operations as representations interpreted by a fuzzy calculus.
- What would challenge FAR: Graded validity cannot be reduced to representation, interpretation, or calculus.

## Causal reasoning

- Why it matters: Intervention and counterfactual dependence may stress the relation between structure and interpretation.
- What would support FAR: A fixture represents causal variables, interventions, and counterfactual transitions using existing primitives.
- What would challenge FAR: Causal intervention requires an irreducible primitive operation outside current calculus and structure.

## Type theory

- Why it matters: Judgments, terms, and types test whether typing relations require distinct primitive treatment.
- What would support FAR: A fixture represents typing judgments and reduction rules as structures and calculus policies.
- What would challenge FAR: Type formation or judgment validity requires a primitive not reducible to current FAR primitives.

## Theorem provers

- Why it matters: Mechanized proof search tests operational sufficiency and auditability at scale.
- What would support FAR: A theorem-prover trace is represented as explicit states, structures, interpretations, and calculus transitions.
- What would challenge FAR: Essential proof-search behavior cannot be made explicit without adding a primitive.

## SAT solving

- Why it matters: SAT solving tests large-scale symbolic search and model construction.
- What would support FAR: Clauses, assignments, propagation, conflict, and learning are represented using existing primitives.
- What would challenge FAR: Search or learned-clause behavior requires an irreducible primitive.

## Category-theoretic reasoning

- Why it matters: Objects, morphisms, functors, and natural transformations test high-abstraction structural reasoning.
- What would support FAR: A fixture represents categorical entities and inference as representational structures with interpreted calculus rules.
- What would challenge FAR: Compositional abstraction requires a primitive not reducible to structure, interpretation, or calculus.
