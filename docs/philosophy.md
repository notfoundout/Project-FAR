# Philosophy

## Purpose

This document describes the research principles that guide the development of Project FAR.

These principles govern how the framework is developed, evaluated, and revised.

They do not constitute claims about reasoning itself.

---

## Explicitness

Project FAR studies reasoning through explicit representations.

Claims, assumptions, evidence, definitions, and reasoning steps should be represented in a form that can be examined and evaluated.

---

## Auditability

Reasoning should be reconstructible from its explicit record.

Another investigator should be able to inspect the reasoning process and determine how a conclusion was reached.

---

## Formalization

Whenever possible, concepts should be defined formally.

Undefined or ambiguous terminology should be eliminated or refined before being used in theoretical claims.

---

## Validation

Every significant claim should be tested against existing reasoning frameworks.

Agreement with existing theories is neither assumed nor rejected in advance.

Validation determines where the proposed architecture succeeds and where it fails.

---

## Falsifiability

The framework should identify conditions under which its claims would be considered false.

Counterexamples are treated as opportunities to improve or revise the theory.

---

## Revision

Project FAR is intended to evolve.

Definitions, architectures, and conjectures may be revised when supported by stronger arguments, formal proofs, or convincing counterexamples.

Major revisions should be documented through the project's version history and decision log.

---

## Minimality

The project seeks the simplest architecture capable of representing reasoning within its stated scope.

Concepts should not be introduced unless they provide explanatory or representational value that cannot be achieved by existing components.
