# Boundary Discovery Program v1.0

## Purpose

This program directs Project FAR toward discovering where its candidate structure fails, becomes underdetermined, or requires additional machinery.

Boundary discovery is not secondary validation. It is a primary route to answering whether any universal structure exists and whether FAR captures it.

## Target classes

The program must actively generate and recruit cases involving:

- self-modifying reasoning;
- nonmonotonic and defeasible reasoning;
- probabilistic and stochastic transitions;
- analogical and relevance-sensitive reasoning;
- embodied and perceptual reasoning;
- distributed and multi-agent reasoning;
- changing semantics and ontology;
- circular and mutually supporting justification;
- tacit expert reasoning;
- reasoning with incomplete or inaccessible internal traces;
- systems that generate new representational types;
- systems whose identity depends on history rather than final state.

## Case lifecycle

Each case proceeds through:

1. source collection;
2. independent reasoning-scope determination;
3. external observation contract construction;
4. protected-distinction review;
5. candidate mapping attempts;
6. preservation and cost evaluation;
7. negative-control execution;
8. classification;
9. claim-registry update;
10. permanent publication of favorable, unfavorable, and unresolved outcomes.

## Classification

Each case must be classified as exactly one of:

- successfully represented;
- representation failure;
- genuine counterexample;
- scope-boundary case;
- interpretation dispute;
- non-reasoning process under independently frozen criteria;
- unresolved;
- theory-revision trigger.

A case may not be excluded by redefining reasoning after its result is known.

## Holdout design

At least one boundary program must use privately held cases controlled by a custodian who does not construct candidate mappings. Theory, semantics, metrics, costs, and failure rules must be frozen before the FAR team sees the holdout cases.

All holdout cases and results must be released after execution, including cases that expose defects in the protocol.

## Counterexample threshold

One genuine in-scope counterexample is sufficient to defeat an unrestricted universality claim. It does not automatically establish that no weaker common structure exists.

## Unknown-boundary registry

Unresolved cases must record:

- missing evidence;
- inaccessible observables;
- mapping disagreements;
- semantic uncertainty;
- implementation limitations;
- what future evidence could resolve the case.

Unknown cases must not be counted as favorable cases.

## Theory revision

When a boundary case requires new theory machinery, the original frozen theory receives the corresponding failure or limitation. The revised theory is tested as a new version.

## Priority rule

After the first prospective pilot, boundary-focused cases take priority over adding further favorable examples of already demonstrated classes.

## Success condition

The program succeeds when it produces a stable, evidence-backed map of where FAR works, where it fails, what remains unknown, and which failures generalize to the central claims.
