# Case and Question
The case is the outbreak of World War I in 1914. The question is whether FAR can represent a concrete historical causal claim without reducing a multi-causal event to one unsupported cause.

# Sources and Scope
Authoritative sources: Annika Mombauer, "July Crisis 1914," 1914-1918 Online, https://encyclopedia.1914-1918-online.net/article/july-crisis-1914-1-1/; Australian Government Anzac Portal, "Origins of World War I," https://anzacportal.dva.gov.au/wars-and-missions/ww1/politics/causes-of-war; National WWI Museum and Memorial exhibition materials on the July Crisis, https://www.theworldwar.org/exhibitions/home-leaves-fall. Scope is outbreak causation in 1914, not long-run war conduct.

# Evidence Inventory
- Source facts: Archduke Franz Ferdinand was assassinated in Sarajevo on 28 June 1914; Austria-Hungary confronted Serbia; Germany supported Austria-Hungary; Russia mobilized; Germany declared war on Russia and France; Britain entered after Germany violated Belgian neutrality.
- Documentary evidence: diplomatic ultimatums, mobilization decisions, alliance commitments, declarations of war, and official communications.
- Interpretations: historians disagree over relative responsibility and whether long-term structures or short-term decisions best explain the outbreak.
- Disputed facts: actor intentions, perceived constraints, and how decision-makers understood escalation risks.

# Explicit Claims
1. The assassination was a triggering event, not by itself a sufficient complete cause.
2. The July Crisis transformed a Balkan crisis into general European war through state decisions.
3. Alliance commitments, military planning, imperial rivalry, nationalism, and perceptions of threat contributed.
4. Causation is overdetermined: several factors were contributing, and removing one may or may not prevent war depending on counterfactual assumptions.

# Reconstructed Argument
A European general war occurred because the assassination created a crisis, Austria-Hungary chose coercion against Serbia, Germany enabled or backed that risk, Russia acted to support Serbia and protect its status, mobilization plans narrowed options, Germany expanded the conflict westward, and Britain entered after Belgian neutrality was violated. Therefore, the outbreak is best represented as a causal chain with interacting structural and decision-level causes.

# Hidden Assumptions
- Diplomatic decisions can be treated as causally significant even when constrained by alliances and military timetables.
- Counterfactual prevention is meaningful but uncertain.
- A cause may be contributing without being individually necessary or sufficient.
- Later knowledge of the war's scale should not be imputed to actors without evidence.

# Inferential Structure
The inference is causal and abductive. It orders events chronologically, identifies mechanisms, evaluates necessary and contributing conditions, and compares causal models. It is not deductive because documentary evidence underdetermines actor intention and counterfactual dependence.

# Alternative Explanations
Competing models emphasize German risk-taking, Austro-Hungarian determination to punish Serbia, Russian mobilization, alliance systems, militarized timetables, imperial competition, nationalism, or accidental escalation. These models can share facts while assigning different causal weights.

# Adversarial Analysis
An adversary could argue that long-term alliance and military structures made war likely regardless of Sarajevo, or that specific short-term decisions made war avoidable. FAR can represent both by making causal dependencies and counterfactual assumptions explicit. FAR cannot assign final historical responsibility without a supplied causal standard.

# Uncertainty and Confidence
Confidence is high for the broad chronology. Confidence is moderate for causal weighting. Uncertainty is high for counterfactual claims, actor intentions where documentary records are incomplete or strategic, and claims that any single factor was necessary.

# FAR Mapping
- Representation: historical causal explanation of a multi-actor event.
- Representational Structure: chronology, evidence, actors, decisions, mechanisms, counterfactuals, causal conclusion.
- Interpretation: historical interpretation of documents, intentions, and structural context.
- Investigation: test candidate causes against chronology and documentary evidence.
- Reasoning Calculus: abductive causal inference with counterfactual sensitivity.
- Operation: construct and compare causal models.
- Evidence: diplomatic chronology, official decisions, authoritative historical syntheses.
- Assumptions: causal relevance standard; counterfactual model; actor rationality and perception model.
- Competing hypotheses: single-trigger model; German-responsibility model; alliance-system model; accidental-escalation model; multi-causal overdetermination model.
- Conclusion: FAR can represent the outbreak as interacting contributing causes rather than one deductive cause.
- Confidence: high for chronology, conditional for causal attribution.
- Dependency structure: documents/chronology -> actor decisions -> mechanisms -> causal model -> weighted conclusion.

# What FAR Clarifies
FAR clarifies the difference between evidence and historical interpretation, between triggers and causes, and between necessary, sufficient, and contributing causes.

# What FAR Does Not Decide
FAR does not determine the correct weighting of German, Austro-Hungarian, Russian, Serbian, British, structural, or accidental causes. It does not eliminate historiographical disagreement.
