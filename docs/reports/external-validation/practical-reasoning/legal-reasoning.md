# Case and Question
Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U.S. 483 (1954), asks whether state-imposed racial segregation in public schools violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The validation question is whether FAR can represent judicial rule application without treating legal authority as logical truth.

# Sources and Scope
Primary source: the United States Reports opinion as provided by the Library of Congress, *Brown v. Board of Education*, 347 U.S. 483 (1954), https://www.loc.gov/item/usrep347483/. Supporting source: National Archives milestone summary, https://www.archives.gov/milestone-documents/brown-v-board-of-education. Scope is the Supreme Court's liability holding, not later remedial proceedings.

# Evidence Inventory
- Source facts: consolidated public-school segregation cases; state laws or practices separated children by race; lower courts varied in treatment; the Court considered education's role and segregation's effect.
- Legal rule: Equal Protection Clause governs state action; prior doctrine included *Plessy v. Ferguson*'s separate-but-equal approach outside education.
- Precedent facts: earlier graduate-school cases had required equality in legal education and graduate education settings.
- Interpretive evidence: the Court reasoned from the social importance of education and the effect of separation on children.
- Disputed or contestable facts: the weight to give social-science evidence and whether original Fourteenth Amendment history controlled the outcome.

# Explicit Claims
1. Public education is a central state function and important to citizenship and opportunity.
2. Segregating children solely by race generates inequality in public education.
3. Separate educational facilities are inherently unequal in this context.
4. State-mandated school segregation denies equal protection.

# Reconstructed Argument
If a state classification denies equal educational opportunity on racial grounds, it violates equal protection. State school segregation classifies students by race and imposes educational inequality because separation itself marks and structures unequal status. Therefore, the segregated school systems at issue violate the Equal Protection Clause.

# Hidden Assumptions
- Equal protection permits courts to assess substantive social meaning, not only formal facility equality.
- Public education's constitutional significance can be interpreted in light of contemporary function.
- Psychological and social effects are legally relevant to equality.
- The precedent framework can be limited or overruled where its rule is incompatible with equal educational protection.

# Inferential Structure
The reasoning is not purely deductive from text alone. It combines authority-dependent rule identification, precedent interpretation, empirical description, normative constitutional interpretation, and judgment about the legal significance of segregated status.

# Alternative Explanations
An alternative reconstruction could hold that materially equal facilities satisfy equality unless constitutional text or original public meaning forbids separation. Another could accept that segregation causes harm but argue that remedy or federalism concerns should shape judicial scope. These alternatives are legally contestable, not resolved by formal logic alone.

# Adversarial Analysis
An adversary could challenge the transition from social harm to constitutional violation, the treatment of *Plessy*, or the use of contemporary education facts. FAR can expose these as premises and dependencies. FAR cannot by itself decide which interpretive theory of the Fourteenth Amendment is authoritative.

# Uncertainty and Confidence
Confidence is high that the opinion concluded state school segregation violates equal protection. Confidence is moderate that the reconstructed premises capture the opinion's operative reasoning because the opinion is brief and deliberately synthetic. Uncertainty remains about deeper interpretive foundations and about how much causal weight the Court placed on social-science materials.

# FAR Mapping
- Representation: judicial opinion and constitutional rule application.
- Representational Structure: legal question, governing rule, case facts, precedent, application, holding.
- Interpretation: constitutional interpretation of equal protection in public education.
- Investigation: identify the rule and test whether facts fall within its prohibition.
- Reasoning Calculus: authority-constrained practical inference, precedent reasoning, analogical and interpretive judgment.
- Operation: classify facts under a constitutional standard.
- Evidence: United States Reports opinion, procedural record summarized in the opinion, precedents cited by the Court.
- Assumptions: equality includes social meaning; education's role matters; precedent can be distinguished or displaced.
- Competing hypotheses: formal equality suffices; separate schooling is inherently unequal; original-history constraint controls.
- Conclusion: segregation in public schools violates equal protection.
- Confidence: high for legal holding, conditional for reconstructed interpretive premises.
- Dependency structure: constitutional text -> judicial authority -> precedent -> factual findings/social meaning -> rule application -> holding.

# What FAR Clarifies
FAR clarifies the distinction between legal authority, empirical premise, interpretive premise, and conclusion. It also clarifies that the holding depends on accepted legal rules and constitutional interpretation rather than on legal authority proving a context-free logical truth.

# What FAR Does Not Decide
FAR does not decide the correct original meaning of the Fourteenth Amendment, the normative legitimacy of judicial review, or the best theory of precedent. It does not prove that a contested constitutional interpretation is substantively true.
