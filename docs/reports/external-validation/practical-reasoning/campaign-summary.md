# Executive Summary
Phase 2 Campaign 5 tested frozen Project FAR Foundation v1.0 against legal reasoning, historical reasoning, intelligence analysis, and public-policy reasoning. All four cases were representable, but only conditionally, because each required domain-specific rules, evidentiary standards, causal models, tradecraft, or normative assumptions.

# Campaign Scope
The campaign created external-validation reports only. It did not modify axioms, definitions, lemmas, propositions, theorems, proofs, proof objects, dependency metadata, doctrine, repository architecture, tooling, or automation.

- Historical reasoning: CONDITIONAL PASS.
- Intelligence analysis: CONDITIONAL PASS.
- Public-policy reasoning: CONDITIONAL PASS.

# Cross-Domain Findings
FAR reliably separated evidence, assumptions, interpretations, inferential steps, competing hypotheses, conclusions, uncertainty, and confidence. It added structure most clearly where practical reasoning mixed facts with authority, causation, probability, or value judgments. It did not determine domain-substantive answers by itself.

# Falsification Attempts
The campaign attempted to falsify representation by using adversarial constitutional interpretation, competing World War I causal models, alternative Iraq WMD hypotheses, and rival minimum-wage mechanisms. These attempts did not show representational failure. They did show that FAR outputs remain conditional on external domain standards.

# Foundation Impact
No Foundation v1.0 artifact changed. The campaign records no need to alter accepted intellectual content, proof objects, dependency metadata, doctrine, architecture, tooling, or automation.

# Limitations
The reports use public and authoritative sources only. They do not settle contested constitutional theory, historiographical responsibility, classified intelligence facts, or optimal public policy. They are reconstructions of reasoning structures, not substantive adjudications.
