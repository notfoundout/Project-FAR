# Case and Question
The case is minimum-wage policy and employment effects. The question is whether FAR can represent a policy-analysis problem with measurable tradeoffs without producing one universal substantive answer.

# Sources and Scope
Authoritative sources: Congressional Budget Office, *The Effects on Employment and Family Income of Increasing the Federal Minimum Wage*, 2019, https://www.cbo.gov/publication/55410; Congressional Budget Office, *The Budgetary and Economic Effects of S. 2488, the Raise the Wage Act of 2023*, 2023, https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2023-12/The_Budgetary_and_Economic_Effects_of_S.%202488_the_Raise_the_Wage_Act_of_2023_1.pdf; U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics minimum-wage worker data, https://www.bls.gov/opub/reports/minimum-wage/. Scope is employment-effects reasoning and tradeoffs, not a complete legislative recommendation.

# Evidence Inventory
- Source facts: federal minimum-wage proposals affect wages for some workers and may affect employment, income distribution, prices, business costs, and federal budget outcomes.
- Empirical evidence types: natural experiments, cross-jurisdiction comparisons, administrative data, surveys, and model-based projections.
- Competing mechanisms: higher wages raise worker income and retention; higher labor costs may reduce employment, hours, hiring, or raise prices.
- Normative premises: poverty reduction, fairness, employment preservation, business viability, and distributional priorities.

# Explicit Claims
1. Minimum-wage policy has both intended wage effects and possible employment or business effects.
2. Evidence can support bounded conclusions conditional on time, place, wage level, labor market, and model assumptions.
3. Positive empirical claims do not by themselves decide normative policy priorities.
4. External validity is limited when findings from one setting are generalized to another.

# Reconstructed Argument
If a minimum-wage increase raises pay for affected workers, it can improve income for workers who remain employed and receive higher wages. If the increase raises labor costs above some employers' willingness or ability to pay, it can reduce employment, hours, hiring, profits, or increase prices. The net policy assessment depends on empirical elasticities, affected population, baseline wages, enforcement, local market conditions, and value judgments about distributional tradeoffs.

# Hidden Assumptions
- Labor demand responses can be estimated from prior evidence or models.
- Workers and firms respond to wage floors in partly predictable ways.
- The policy objective is not merely efficiency but also distributional improvement.
- A national estimate may mask heterogeneous local effects.

# Inferential Structure
The reasoning combines causal modeling, empirical synthesis, projection, distributional analysis, and normative evaluation. It is not deductive because the empirical parameters and value weights are uncertain and context dependent.

# Alternative Explanations
Observed employment changes may result from macroeconomic trends, sectoral shifts, enforcement variation, monopsony power, productivity changes, or unrelated local shocks. Wage gains may reflect policy, tight labor markets, employer adaptation, or compositional changes.

# Adversarial Analysis
An adversary could argue that employment losses outweigh wage gains or that monopsony and turnover effects make losses small. Another adversary could argue that even small employment losses are normatively unacceptable, while a defender could prioritize aggregate income gains for low-wage workers. FAR can represent these premises but cannot choose value weights.

# Uncertainty and Confidence
Confidence is high that minimum-wage increases involve tradeoffs among wages, employment, prices, and business effects. Confidence varies for magnitude estimates because they depend on proposal level, affected labor market, methodology, and time horizon. Uncertainty is high when extrapolating beyond observed wage increases.

# FAR Mapping
- Representation: public-policy causal and evaluative analysis.
- Representational Structure: objective, target population, causal model, evidence, mechanisms, tradeoffs, conclusion.
- Interpretation: empirical and normative interpretation of labor-market evidence.
- Investigation: assess evidence for wage, employment, price, and distributional effects.
- Reasoning Calculus: causal inference, model-based projection, cost-benefit and distributional reasoning.
- Operation: compare policy outcomes under explicit assumptions.
- Evidence: CBO estimates, BLS data, research summaries incorporated by CBO.
- Assumptions: labor-market model; external validity; normative policy objective; enforcement conditions.
- Competing hypotheses: disemployment effect; small/no employment effect; monopsony-mediated wage gains; price pass-through; business adaptation.
- Conclusion: FAR can represent bounded policy reasoning when empirical model and normative premises are explicit.
- Confidence: conditional and context-sensitive.
- Dependency structure: policy design -> target population -> causal model -> empirical parameter estimates -> tradeoff analysis -> normative weighting -> policy conclusion.

# What FAR Clarifies
FAR clarifies positive versus normative premises, causal mechanisms, evidence type, external validity, uncertainty, and tradeoff dependencies.

# What FAR Does Not Decide
FAR does not decide the socially optimal minimum wage, the correct welfare weights, or a universal employment effect. It does not replace empirical labor economics or democratic value choice.
