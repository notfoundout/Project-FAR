# External Validation: Biology

## Case Study

Mendelian inheritance provides a canonical case of controlled crosses, discrete trait categories, predicted offspring ratios, and inference from repeated observations.

## FAR Mapping

| FAR role | Scientific realization |
| --- | --- |
| Representation | parental trait, gamete type, offspring phenotype, count, ratio |
| Representational structure | cross design, generation, dominance relation, contingency table |
| Interpretation | observed trait categories mapped to an inheritance model |
| Investigation | test segregation or dominance predictions |
| Reasoning calculus | combinatorics, probability, experimental controls, statistical comparison |
| Operation | crossing, counting, classification, ratio calculation, model comparison |

## Valid Inference

Under a single-locus dominant/recessive model, crossing two heterozygotes predicts genotype proportions 1:2:1 and phenotype proportions 3:1, subject to sampling variation.

FAR can represent the parental assumptions, combinatorial structure, predicted distribution, observed counts, and comparison operation.

Result: PASS.

## Invalid Inference

A small offspring sample close to 3:1 is treated as proof that the trait is always controlled by one locus with complete dominance.

This confuses model compatibility with unique causal identification and ignores alternative genetic mechanisms and sampling error.

Result: PASS.

## Malformed or Scope-Violating Case

Phenotype categories are changed after observing the data, or generations and crosses are pooled without preserving their experimental structure.

FAR can identify that the representation and investigation changed, invalidating a direct comparison with the original prediction.

Result: PASS.

## Alternative Explanations and Limits

Departures from simple ratios may arise from linkage, incomplete dominance, penetrance, viability effects, environmental influence, classification error, or sampling variation.

## Source Basis

Mendel's pea-cross experiments are the historical basis for discrete inheritance models and segregation reasoning. This case uses the simplified single-locus model as an explicit hypothesis, not as a universal account of heredity.

## Final Outcome

CONDITIONAL PASS

Condition: the inheritance model, cross design, category definitions, sampling assumptions, and comparison method must be explicit.
