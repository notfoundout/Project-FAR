# Philosophy External Validation Campaign Method

## Purpose

This campaign tests whether the frozen Project FAR Foundation v1.0 can represent and evaluate philosophical arguments without collapsing logical structure into agreement with the argument's premises.

The campaign will analyze representative arguments from:

- Plato;
- Aristotle;
- Hume;
- Kant;
- Popper.

The campaign is interpretive and comparative. It does not treat contested philosophical premises as established facts.

## Frozen Baseline

Foundation v1.0 is fixed. No axiom, definition, lemma, proposition, theorem, proof, dependency, or doctrine may be changed in this branch.

## Evaluation Questions

Each philosopher-specific report must identify:

1. the exact argument selected;
2. the primary text or clearly identified source passage;
3. the argument's explicit premises;
4. any reconstructed or hidden premises;
5. the conclusion;
6. the inferential structure;
7. the relevant interpretation and investigation scope;
8. whether the argument is deductive, inductive, abductive, transcendental, dialectical, or mixed;
9. whether the inference is valid, strong, weak, question-begging, underdetermined, or otherwise limited;
10. what FAR clarifies;
11. what FAR does not decide.

## Source Discipline

Each report must be grounded in a specific primary argument or passage. Secondary scholarship may be used only to identify interpretive disputes or alternative reconstructions.

No report may present a disputed reconstruction as the philosopher's uniquely correct argument.

Direct quotation should remain minimal. The reports should primarily paraphrase and formally reconstruct the reasoning.

## Argument Reconstruction Standard

Each report must separate:

- textual claims;
- interpretive reconstruction;
- formalized premises;
- hidden assumptions;
- conclusion;
- validity or inferential-strength assessment;
- soundness or premise-acceptability assessment.

Where multiple defensible reconstructions exist, the report must state which reconstruction is being tested and record the alternatives.

## FAR Mapping

Each case maps the argument into:

| FAR role | Philosophical realization |
| --- | --- |
| Representation | proposition, distinction, definition, example, objection, conclusion |
| Representational structure | premise-conclusion graph, dialogue structure, dependency chain, dilemma, reductio |
| Interpretation | meanings assigned to the argument's terms and distinctions |
| Investigation | the philosophical question being answered |
| Reasoning calculus | deductive rules, inductive standards, dialectical procedure, or argument-specific method |
| Operation | definition, distinction, inference, counterexample, abstraction, generalization, reductio |

## Test Pattern

Each philosopher-specific report must include:

- one charitable reconstruction;
- one adversarial reconstruction or objection;
- one hidden-assumption analysis;
- one scope or ambiguity test;
- one explicit statement of what FAR cannot resolve.

## Outcome Classes

- PASS: FAR represents and distinguishes the tested argument's relevant structure without foundational change.
- CONDITIONAL PASS: FAR succeeds only after a disputed interpretation, premise set, or inferential standard is supplied explicitly.
- FAIL: FAR cannot represent a required feature without changing Foundation v1.0.
- INCONCLUSIVE: the available text or interpretive evidence does not support a stable reconstruction.

## Non-Claims

A successful mapping does not establish that:

- the philosopher's conclusion is true;
- the selected interpretation is uniquely correct;
- FAR resolves the philosophical dispute;
- FAR is superior to ordinary philosophical analysis;
- one case represents the philosopher's entire system;
- the campaign proves universal philosophical applicability.

## Planned Cases

### Plato

Primary target: the Euthyphro dilemma concerning whether the pious is loved by the gods because it is pious or is pious because it is loved.

Focus:

- dilemma structure;
- dependence and explanation;
- ambiguity between recognition and constitution;
- whether the dilemma exhausts the alternatives.

### Aristotle

Primary target: a representative categorical syllogism and Aristotle's treatment of valid syllogistic form.

Focus:

- term structure;
- universal and particular claims;
- validity independent of premise truth;
- dependence on quantified interpretation.

### Hume

Primary target: the problem of induction from the Enquiry concerning the justification of inference from observed to unobserved cases.

Focus:

- demonstrative versus probable reasoning;
- circularity in appeals to past uniformity;
- hidden uniformity assumptions;
- scope of skeptical conclusion.

### Kant

Primary target: a representative transcendental argument, provisionally the argument that experience requires certain a priori conditions.

Focus:

- regressive or transcendental inference;
- necessity claims;
- alternative conditions;
- distinction between conditions of experience and conditions of reality.

The exact passage must be fixed in the Kant commit before final evaluation.

### Popper

Primary target: falsifiability as a criterion of demarcation and the asymmetry between verification and falsification.

Focus:

- universal statements and counterinstances;
- logical versus methodological falsification;
- auxiliary assumptions;
- whether falsifiability alone resolves demarcation.

## Final Campaign Decision Rule

The campaign summary may conclude only one of:

- PHILOSOPHY CAMPAIGN PASSED;
- PHILOSOPHY CAMPAIGN CONDITIONALLY PASSED;
- PHILOSOPHY CAMPAIGN FAILED;
- PHILOSOPHY CAMPAIGN INCONCLUSIVE.

No final campaign status will be assigned until all planned cases are completed and compared.
