# Primary Argument

Plato's target argument is the Euthyphro dilemma in *Euthyphro*, conventionally focused on 10a-11b: is the pious loved by the gods because it is pious, or is it pious because it is loved by the gods?

# Source and Scope

Source: Plato, *Euthyphro*, translated by Benjamin Jowett, public-domain text available through Project Gutenberg. The report uses the dilemma passage only and does not treat the whole dialogue as a complete theory of piety.

The scope is explanatory dependence: whether divine love recognizes piety or constitutes it.

# Textual Claims

Socrates asks Euthyphro to distinguish the property of being pious from the condition of being loved by the gods. The passage treats being loved as something that happens to an object and piety as the feature that may explain why the object is loved.

Euthyphro's earlier definition also creates pressure from divine disagreement: if gods disagree about what is just or unjust, the same act may be loved and hated by different gods.

# Charitable Reconstruction

A charitable reconstruction treats the dilemma as a test of a proposed definition, not as a complete refutation of all divine-command theories.

If an act is loved by the gods because it is pious, then piety is explanatorily prior to divine approval and cannot be identical with being god-loved. If an act is pious because it is loved by the gods, then divine approval appears to constitute piety, but the account must explain why approval is not arbitrary and how disagreement among gods is handled.

# Formalized Premises

P1. Euthyphro proposes that pious acts are acts loved by the gods.

P2. For any act A, either divine love of A depends on A's piety, or A's piety depends on divine love of A.

P3. If divine love depends on A's piety, then being god-loved is not what constitutes piety.

P4. If A's piety depends on divine love, then piety is constituted by divine attitude and requires a rule for divine agreement and non-arbitrariness.

P5. Euthyphro's account does not supply that rule.

C. The proposed definition does not successfully identify piety with being loved by the gods.

# Hidden Assumptions

- Explanatory priority and identity cannot run in opposite directions without further account.
- Moral or religious properties can be distinguished from attitudes toward them.
- The gods' attitudes are representable as a coherent source of classification.
- Divine disagreement is possible or at least not ruled out by Euthyphro's definition.
- A definition of piety must state what makes pious acts pious, not merely a reliable sign of piety.

# Inferential Structure

The structure is a dilemma with an explanatory-dependence test. The argument is not simply: either gods love piety or piety is god-loved. It tests whether the proposed definition confuses constitution with recognition.

The inference is strong against Euthyphro's specific definition if P2 is accepted as exhaustive and if definition requires constitution rather than coextension. It is weaker against theories that identify divine command with a unified divine nature, restrict the gods to one perfectly good deity, or distinguish divine recognition from divine legislation.

# Adversarial Reconstruction

An adversarial reconstruction denies that the alternatives are exhaustive. A theory might hold that divine willing and moral goodness are necessarily coextensive without a temporal or causal priority relation. Another might claim that piety is constituted by the will of a perfectly rational divine being whose nature is not arbitrary.

Under that reconstruction, Socrates exposes an unargued ambiguity in Euthyphro's answer but does not refute every possible theological ethics.

# Ambiguity and Scope Tests

- If “loved by the gods” means all gods unanimously love the act, the disagreement objection is weakened but not eliminated.
- If “because” means causal production, the dilemma differs from an account of metaphysical grounding.
- If “piety” is a social-religious status rather than a moral property, constitution by divine attitude becomes more plausible.
- If divine recognition is treated as perfect evidence, then god-loved may be a sign of piety without being its essence.

# FAR Mapping

- Representation: acts, divine attitudes, piety predicates, and explanatory relations.
- Representational Structure: dilemma with two directed dependence relations.
- Interpretation: “because” is interpreted as explanatory or grounding priority, not mere chronology.
- Investigation: test whether the proposed definition identifies the essence of piety.
- Reasoning Calculus: disjunctive reasoning plus identity and dependence constraints.
- Operation: distinguish constitution from recognition and test each horn.
- Evidence: the Euthyphro passage and Euthyphro's proposed definition.
- Assumptions: exhaustiveness of the horns, divine-attitude coherence, and a constitutive standard for definition.
- Conclusion: Euthyphro's definition is unstable unless additional assumptions are supplied.
- Dependency structure: conclusion depends on the textual definition, the interpretation of “because,” and the hidden assumptions about definition and divine agreement.

# What FAR Clarifies

FAR clarifies that the dispute is not just about a sentence's wording. The decisive dependencies are the direction of explanation, the distinction between property and recognition, and the hidden premise that a definition must be constitutive.

FAR also shows where the dilemma is text-grounded and where later divine-command reconstructions add premises not supplied by Euthyphro.

# What FAR Does Not Decide

FAR does not decide whether piety exists, whether divine-command ethics is true, whether moral properties are independent of God, or whether a later monotheistic theory can escape the dilemma.
