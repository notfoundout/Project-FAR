# Primary Argument

Aristotle's target is a representative categorical syllogism from *Prior Analytics* I.4, the first-figure pattern later called Barbara: if A is predicated of every B, and B is predicated of every C, then A is predicated of every C.

# Source and Scope

Source: Aristotle, *Prior Analytics*, translated by A. J. Jenkinson in the public-domain Oxford Aristotle translation. The scope is categorical syllogistic validity, not Aristotle's complete theory of demonstration.

The representative instance is: all humans are mortal; all Greeks are humans; therefore all Greeks are mortal.

# Textual Claims

Aristotle characterizes a syllogism as discourse in which, certain things being supposed, something different follows of necessity because of those suppositions. In the first figure, the middle term links the subject class to the predicate class.

The passage distinguishes the form of following from the truth of particular premises.

# Charitable Reconstruction

The argument is valid because membership in the minor class is included in the middle class, and membership in the middle class is included in the major class. If every C is B and every B is A, then every C is A.

The syllogism's validity is independent of whether the terms chosen in a concrete example are empirically true.

# Formalized Premises

P1. For all x, if x is Greek, then x is human.

P2. For all x, if x is human, then x is mortal.

C. For all x, if x is Greek, then x is mortal.

Term roles:

- Major term: mortal.
- Minor term: Greek.
- Middle term: human.
- Major premise: all humans are mortal.
- Minor premise: all Greeks are humans.

# Hidden Assumptions

- The same term keeps the same meaning across premises.
- Universal predication is interpreted extensionally as class inclusion or conditionals over objects.
- The domain of quantification is stable across premises.
- The syllogism is evaluated for validity, not for empirical soundness.
- Empty-term issues are handled by the chosen quantified interpretation.

# Inferential Structure

The structure is deductive. Under modern universal quantification, the conclusion follows by hypothetical syllogism and universal generalization.

A particular variant would require an existential premise, for example: some Greeks are humans; all humans are mortal; therefore some Greeks are mortal. That form is also valid if the particular premise supplies existence.

# Adversarial Reconstruction

An invalid case arises if the middle term equivocates: all bank employees are financial workers; all river banks are banks; therefore all river banks are financial workers. The surface form appears syllogistic, but the middle term does not preserve one interpretation.

Another invalid case arises from undistributed middle: all dogs are animals; all cats are animals; therefore all cats are dogs. The common predicate does not link the minor class through inclusion in the major class.

# Ambiguity and Scope Tests

- If universals have existential import, some traditional inferences differ from modern first-order readings.
- If terms shift meaning, formal validity is not preserved.
- If the domain changes between premises, the middle term cannot mediate the inference.
- If premises are false but form is Barbara, validity remains intact while soundness fails.

# FAR Mapping

- Representation: categorical terms, quantified claims, premises, and conclusion.
- Representational Structure: first-figure syllogism with a middle term connecting minor and major terms.
- Interpretation: universal predication is interpreted as class inclusion or universal conditional.
- Investigation: determine whether the conclusion follows from the premises by form.
- Reasoning Calculus: syllogistic rule Barbara, or modern universal-instantiation and conditional-transitivity rules.
- Operation: map terms, preserve identity of the middle term, and test the conclusion.
- Evidence: *Prior Analytics* I.4 and the representative syllogism.
- Assumptions: stable meanings, stable domain, and selected treatment of existential import.
- Conclusion: the valid form is representable; invalid equivocation and distribution failures are identifiable.
- Dependency structure: validity depends on form and term identity; soundness depends separately on premise truth.

# What FAR Clarifies

FAR clarifies the separation between validity and premise truth. It also makes term roles explicit and exposes equivocation as a representation failure, not merely a bad conclusion.

# What FAR Does Not Decide

FAR does not decide Aristotle's broader metaphysics of predication, whether traditional existential import should be preferred, or whether syllogistic is adequate for all valid reasoning.
