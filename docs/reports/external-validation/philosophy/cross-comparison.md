# Philosophy External Validation Cross Comparison

## Overview

The five cases test FAR against different forms of philosophical reasoning: dilemma, syllogism, skeptical eliminative argument, transcendental argument, and methodological-logical demarcation.

## Comparison Matrix

| Case | Argument type | Hidden premises | Deductive or non-deductive structure | Dependence on interpretation | Outcome |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Plato | Dilemma and explanatory-dependence test | exhaustiveness, divine agreement, definition as constitution | partly deductive, partly explanatory | high | CONDITIONAL PASS |
| Aristotle | categorical syllogism | stable terms, domain, existential import convention | deductive | moderate | CONDITIONAL PASS |
| Hume | skeptical eliminative argument | exhaustive warrant types, anti-circularity, uniformity requirement | deductive if warrant classification is accepted | high | CONDITIONAL PASS |
| Kant | transcendental argument | objective experience, insufficiency of sensation, uniqueness | regressive and modal | very high | CONDITIONAL PASS |
| Popper | logical asymmetry plus methodological norm | basic-statement acceptance, auxiliary handling, demarcation standard | deductive for asymmetry; non-deductive for method | high | CONDITIONAL PASS |

## Argument Type

Plato tests a dilemma organized by explanatory priority. Aristotle tests formal validity in a categorical structure. Hume tests whether possible justifications are exhausted and fail. Kant tests whether a phenomenon has necessary enabling conditions. Popper combines a formal point about universals with a methodological criterion for science.

## Role of Hidden Premises

Hidden premises are decisive in all five cases. Aristotle has the least disputed hidden premise set when treated as a formal syllogism, but even there term stability and existential import matter. Plato, Hume, Kant, and Popper require explicit assumptions before the argument's force can be evaluated.

## Deductive Versus Non-Deductive Structure

Aristotle is the clearest deductive case. Hume becomes deductive only after accepting the demonstrative/probable classification and a strict standard of justification. Popper's falsification asymmetry is deductive, while his demarcation criterion is methodological. Plato and Kant involve explanatory or transcendental structures that cannot be reduced to simple validity tests without interpretive loss.

## Dependence on Interpretation

All cases depend on interpretation. Plato depends on the meaning of “because” and the status of piety. Aristotle depends on the interpretation of categorical predication. Hume depends on the scope of rational justification. Kant depends on the meaning of objective experience and a priori conditions. Popper depends on the relation between logical falsification and scientific method.

## Validity Versus Soundness

FAR preserves the distinction between validity and soundness across the campaign. Aristotle shows the distinction most directly. Hume and Popper show that valid substructures can support conclusions whose soundness depends on contested methodological premises. Plato and Kant show that explanatory or transcendental validity requires separate scrutiny of the reconstructed dependency relation.

## Explanatory Versus Logical Dependence

Plato and Kant are the central explanatory-dependence cases. Plato asks whether divine love constitutes or recognizes piety. Kant asks whether objective experience depends on a priori structuring conditions. Aristotle is primarily logical. Hume and Popper combine logical relations with epistemic or methodological dependence.

## Scope of Conclusion

The campaign repeatedly narrows overstrong conclusions. Plato undermines Euthyphro's definition, not every theology. Aristotle validates a form, not every premise. Hume challenges non-circular rational justification, not practical induction. Kant supports at most the need for conditions of objective experience unless uniqueness is separately shown. Popper establishes asymmetry and testability, not a complete theory of science.

## Adversarial Robustness

FAR supported adversarial testing in each case by making attack points explicit:

- Plato: non-exhaustive horns or refined divine-command theory;
- Aristotle: equivocation, undistributed middle, and existential-import disputes;
- Hume: pragmatic, Bayesian, reliabilist, or rule-circular replies;
- Kant: alternative enabling conditions and uniqueness objections;
- Popper: auxiliary assumptions, theory-ladenness, and insufficiency of falsifiability.

## Where FAR Adds Structure

FAR adds structure by forcing each report to separate representation, interpretation, assumptions, evidence, operations, conclusion, and dependencies. This is most useful where philosophical arguments mix textual claims with reconstructed premises.

## Where FAR Adds No Substantive Philosophical Answer

FAR does not decide the truth of piety, the correct account of syllogistic existential import, the ultimate justification of induction, the success of Kant's deduction, or the sufficiency of Popper's demarcation criterion. It improves auditability, not substantive philosophical resolution.
