# Primary Argument

Hume's target argument is the problem of induction in *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*, Section IV, “Sceptical Doubts concerning the Operations of the Understanding.”

# Source and Scope

Source: David Hume, *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*, public-domain editions commonly based on the 1777 text. The report addresses Hume's argument that inferences from observed to unobserved matters of fact are not justified by demonstrative reasoning or by non-circular probable reasoning.

The scope is epistemic warrant for induction, not a denial that humans naturally form inductive expectations.

# Textual Claims

Hume distinguishes relations of ideas from matters of fact. Demonstrative reasoning concerns contradictions and necessity; probable reasoning concerns experience and causal expectation.

When we infer that the future will resemble the past, the inference cannot be demonstrative because the contrary is conceivable. It also cannot be justified by probable reasoning without assuming the very regularity that induction is supposed to justify.

# Charitable Reconstruction

The charitable reconstruction treats Hume as challenging rational justification, not practical habit. Past regularity supports future expectation only if some uniformity principle connects observed and unobserved cases. But that principle is not demonstrable and cannot be established by appeal to past success without circularity.

# Formalized Premises

P1. Reasoning about unobserved matters of fact depends on causal or inductive inference.

P2. A rational justification for such inference must be either demonstrative or probable.

P3. Demonstrative justification fails because the negation of future resemblance to the past is conceivable without contradiction.

P4. Probable justification appeals to past regularity or prior inductive success.

P5. Appealing to past regularity to justify induction presupposes that past regularity is a guide to unobserved cases.

C. Inductive inference lacks a non-circular rational justification of the demanded kind.

# Hidden Assumptions

- The demonstrative/probable distinction is exhaustive for the relevant justification.
- Conceivability without contradiction is enough to rule out demonstrative necessity.
- Circular justification is not acceptable as foundational rational warrant.
- The uniformity-of-nature assumption is required for observed-to-unobserved inference.
- The target is justification, not psychological explanation or pragmatic indispensability.

# Inferential Structure

The argument is a negative epistemological argument by elimination. It divides candidate justifications into demonstrative and probable forms, rejects the first by non-contradiction, and rejects the second by circularity.

The inference is deductively strong if the classification of possible justifications is accepted. It is less decisive if one allows pragmatic, reliabilist, Bayesian, or naturalized standards that do not fit Hume's demanded justification.

# Adversarial Reconstruction

An adversarial reconstruction rejects P2's exhaustiveness. It may argue that inductive norms are justified pragmatically, by inference to the best explanation, by Bayesian coherence plus updating rules, or by a naturalized account of reliable belief formation.

Another adversarial reconstruction accepts circularity but treats some rule-circular justification as unavoidable and legitimate.

# Ambiguity and Scope Tests

- Hume's conclusion does not show that induction is false.
- It does not show that inductive habits are psychologically optional.
- It does not show that all probabilistic reasoning is useless.
- It targets the rational foundation of observed-to-unobserved inference under a strict standard.
- The strength of the argument changes if “justification” is pragmatic rather than demonstrative or evidential.

# FAR Mapping

- Representation: observed cases, unobserved cases, regularities, inferential rules, and justification types.
- Representational Structure: eliminative argument over candidate warrant classes.
- Interpretation: “reason” is interpreted as rational justification, not psychological cause.
- Investigation: determine whether induction has a non-circular justification.
- Reasoning Calculus: disjunction elimination, contradiction test, and circularity detection.
- Operation: classify warrant types and test each for adequacy.
- Evidence: Section IV's distinction between demonstrative and probable reasoning.
- Assumptions: exhaustiveness of warrant types, rejection of circularity, and need for uniformity.
- Conclusion: induction lacks the specified non-circular rational justification.
- Dependency structure: conclusion depends on Hume's classification of reasoning and the hidden standard for justification.

# What FAR Clarifies

FAR clarifies the exact scope of the skeptical conclusion. The argument undermines one kind of justificatory claim; it does not license the stronger claim that inductive practice should cease or that future regularity is impossible.

FAR also separates the uniformity assumption from the empirical observations it is used to project.

# What FAR Does Not Decide

FAR does not decide whether Bayesian, pragmatic, evolutionary, or reliabilist replies succeed. It does not decide whether nature is uniform or whether induction is practically unavoidable.
