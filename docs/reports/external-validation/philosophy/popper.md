# Primary Argument

Popper's target argument is falsifiability as a demarcation criterion and the logical asymmetry between verification and falsification in *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*.

# Source and Scope

Source: Karl Popper, *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*, especially the early sections on the problem of demarcation and falsifiability. The report uses the argument about universal statements and counterinstances, not Popper's entire philosophy of science.

The scope includes logical falsification and methodological complications involving auxiliary assumptions and observational practice.

# Textual Claims

Popper argues that universal scientific theories cannot be conclusively verified by any finite number of positive instances. A universal statement can, however, conflict with a singular basic statement reporting a counterinstance.

He also treats falsification as methodological, because actual testing involves decisions about basic statements, auxiliary hypotheses, instruments, and background assumptions.

# Charitable Reconstruction

A charitable reconstruction separates a logical point from a methodological rule. Logically, “all swans are white” is contradicted by “there exists a non-white swan.” Methodologically, deciding that an observed case is a genuine counterinstance requires background commitments.

Falsifiability is therefore a criterion of empirical testability, not an automatic machine for rejecting theories in isolation.

# Formalized Premises

P1. Universal empirical theories have the form: for all x, if x is F, then x is G.

P2. Any finite set of positive instances is logically compatible with a future or unobserved counterinstance.

P3. A single accepted counterinstance of the form F and not G contradicts the universal statement.

P4. Scientific theories should expose themselves to possible empirical refutation.

P5. Actual falsification requires methodological decisions about observation statements and auxiliary assumptions.

C. Falsifiability marks an important condition of scientific testability, but falsification in practice is mediated by methodological commitments.

# Hidden Assumptions

- Universal theories can be represented in a form admitting counterinstances.
- Basic statements can be accepted sufficiently for testing, even if fallible.
- Demarcation concerns empirical science rather than meaningfulness or truth.
- Auxiliary hypotheses can be distinguished enough to locate test conflict.
- A good demarcation criterion need not by itself solve theory choice.

# Inferential Structure

The logical asymmetry is deductive: universal generalization is not entailed by finitely many positive instances, while a universal statement is contradicted by a counterinstance.

The demarcation claim is methodological and normative. It does not follow solely from the logic of quantifiers; it adds the rule that scientific theories should be exposed to risky tests.

# Adversarial Reconstruction

An adversarial reconstruction emphasizes Duhem-Quine problems: a failed prediction tests a conjunction of theory, auxiliaries, initial conditions, and instrumentation. Therefore, no theory is falsified alone by observation.

Another adversarial reconstruction argues that falsifiability is not sufficient for science because astrology-like or ad hoc systems may generate testable claims, while mature sciences may protect core theories temporarily through principled auxiliary revisions.

# Ambiguity and Scope Tests

- Logical falsification is clearer than methodological falsification.
- Falsifiability is not the same as actual falsity.
- A theory can be falsifiable yet poorly confirmed, ad hoc, or scientifically weak.
- Observation is theory-laden, so basic statements are not neutral givens.
- The criterion addresses demarcation but does not fully resolve acceptance, pursuit, or explanation.

# FAR Mapping

- Representation: universal statements, singular basic statements, counterinstances, auxiliary assumptions, and tests.
- Representational Structure: contradiction relation plus methodological dependency graph.
- Interpretation: “falsifiable” is interpreted as prohibiting some possible observations, not as already falsified.
- Investigation: determine whether a theory has empirical risk and how a failed test is allocated.
- Reasoning Calculus: first-order contradiction for universal claims and methodological rules for test appraisal.
- Operation: compare theory predictions with accepted basic statements and inspect auxiliaries.
- Evidence: Popper's demarcation and falsifiability discussion in *The Logic of Scientific Discovery*.
- Assumptions: acceptability of basic statements, auxiliary management, and normative demarcation standard.
- Conclusion: FAR can represent the logical asymmetry and the methodological dependencies, but falsifiability alone does not settle all demarcation questions.
- Dependency structure: logical falsification depends on formal contradiction; methodological falsification depends on background decisions and accepted observations.

# What FAR Clarifies

FAR clarifies the difference between the formal contradiction that defeats a universal statement and the methodological decision to treat an observation as a decisive test result.

It also exposes why auxiliary assumptions and theory-laden observations are not peripheral; they are dependencies of the falsification claim.

# What FAR Does Not Decide

FAR does not decide whether Popper's demarcation criterion is sufficient, how scientific communities should handle every auxiliary revision, or whether a particular historical theory should be abandoned.
