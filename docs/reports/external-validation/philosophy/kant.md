# Primary Argument

Kant's target argument is the transcendental claim in the *Critique of Pure Reason* that objective experience requires a priori conditions supplied by the understanding, especially in the Transcendental Deduction of the categories.

# Source and Scope

Source: Immanuel Kant, *Critique of Pure Reason*, translated by J. M. D. Meiklejohn, public-domain text. The report focuses on the argument that objects of experience must be synthesizable under rules or categories of the understanding.

The scope is conditions of possible experience, not conditions of things as they are in themselves.

# Textual Claims

Kant distinguishes appearances from things in themselves and argues that experience is not a mere stream of sensations. Objective experience requires unity, synthesis, and rule-governed connection supplied by the understanding.

The categories are presented as a priori concepts that make experience of objects possible.

# Charitable Reconstruction

A charitable reconstruction treats the argument as regressive or transcendental: begin with the fact that we have objective experience, ask what must be true for such experience to be possible, and infer that some a priori structuring conditions are necessary.

The argument does not infer from psychology alone. It claims that object-directed experience has formal requirements that cannot be derived merely from isolated sensations.

# Formalized Premises

P1. We have objective experience, meaning experience represented as experience of objects under unified relations.

P2. Objective experience requires synthesis of representations into a rule-governed unity.

P3. Rule-governed unity cannot be supplied by isolated sensory manifold alone.

P4. The understanding supplies a priori rules or categories that make such unity possible.

C. Objective experience requires a priori conditions supplied by the understanding.

# Hidden Assumptions

- Objective experience is genuinely present and not merely presumed by ordinary cognition.
- Conditions of representational objectivity can be identified by transcendental analysis.
- Sensibility alone cannot supply the relevant unity.
- The proposed categories are necessary conditions rather than contingent cognitive habits.
- The inference from required conditions to Kant's specific categories is legitimate.

# Inferential Structure

The structure is transcendental rather than ordinary deductive derivation from empirical premises. It proceeds from an accepted phenomenon to claimed necessary conditions for that phenomenon.

The necessity claim is strong only if P2 and P3 are accepted and if no alternative condition can explain objective experience equally well.

# Adversarial Reconstruction

An adversarial reconstruction accepts that objective experience requires structure but denies that Kant has established the uniqueness of his categories. Alternative candidates include linguistic structures, biological cognition, learned conceptual schemes, probabilistic predictive processing, or social practices of object identification.

Another adversarial reconstruction challenges the starting point by treating “objective experience” as a constructed or fallible achievement rather than an unquestioned fact.

# Ambiguity and Scope Tests

- If the argument establishes only that some structure is required, it does not establish Kant's table of categories.
- If “a priori” means strictly necessary for any possible experiencer, empirical cognitive alternatives matter less.
- If “a priori” means prior in our human cognitive constitution, the claim is narrower.
- The argument concerns experience of objects, not knowledge of things in themselves.
- The argument's force changes if appearances are not interpreted in Kant's technical sense.

# FAR Mapping

- Representation: experiences, appearances, objects, synthesis, rules, categories, and conditions.
- Representational Structure: transcendental dependency from objective experience to enabling conditions.
- Interpretation: “condition” is interpreted as a condition of possible experience, not a cause among objects.
- Investigation: determine what must be presupposed for objective experience.
- Reasoning Calculus: regressive transcendental reasoning with necessity and alternative-condition tests.
- Operation: identify the target phenomenon, infer candidate conditions, and test uniqueness.
- Evidence: the Transcendental Deduction's account of synthesis and categories.
- Assumptions: objective experience, insufficiency of sensation alone, and validity of transcendental method.
- Conclusion: objective experience requires some a priori structuring by understanding; stronger claims about Kant's exact categories require more argument.
- Dependency structure: the conclusion depends on the interpretation of objective experience, conditions, a priori status, and uniqueness.

# What FAR Clarifies

FAR clarifies that the argument has multiple layers: a general condition claim, a claim that the condition is supplied by understanding, and a stronger claim that Kant's specific categories are uniquely necessary.

FAR also separates conditions of experience from claims about things in themselves.

# What FAR Does Not Decide

FAR does not decide whether Kant's deduction succeeds, whether the categories are unique, whether transcendental idealism is true, or whether later naturalized accounts defeat the argument.
