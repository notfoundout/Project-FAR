# Example — Disagreement Analysis

## Purpose

This example demonstrates how the Foundational Analysis of Reasoning Operations (FARO) identifies the source of disagreement between two reasoning processes.

The objective is not to determine which conclusion is correct, but to explain why different conclusions were reached.

---

# Investigation

Two investigators evaluate the same question.

**Question**

Should a city construct a new bridge?

Both investigators have access to the same engineering report.

However, they reach different conclusions.

---

# Reasoning Process A

## Resolution

Construct the bridge.

### Primary Considerations

- Existing bridge is approaching capacity.
- Engineering report predicts increased traffic.
- Long-term economic benefits outweigh construction costs.

---

# Reasoning Process B

## Resolution

Do not construct the bridge.

### Primary Considerations

- Construction cost exceeds current budget.
- Existing bridge remains structurally safe.
- Alternative transportation improvements are available.

---

# Comparison

Both investigations share:

- The same investigation.
- The same engineering report.
- The same objective.

They differ in how the available evidence is interpreted and prioritized.

---

# Disagreement Analysis

## Step 1 — Common Representations

Both reasoning processes use:

- Engineering report
- Traffic projections
- Budget estimates

---

## Step 2 — Common Reasoning Calculus

Both investigations employ practical policy reasoning.

---

## Step 3 — Earliest Divergence

The first significant divergence occurs during interpretation.

Reasoning Process A interprets future traffic demand as the dominant consideration.

Reasoning Process B interprets current financial constraints as the dominant consideration.

---

## Step 4 — Propagation

The differing interpretations produce different admissibility classifications for the available candidates.

As a result, each investigation applies the same resolution rule to different admissibility structures.

---

# Candidates

- Construct the bridge.
- Do not construct the bridge.

---

# Admissibility Structure (Ω)

### Reasoning Process A

| Candidate | Admissibility |
|-----------|---------------|
| Construct the bridge | Admissible |
| Do not construct the bridge | Not Admissible |

### Reasoning Process B

| Candidate | Admissibility |
|-----------|---------------|
| Construct the bridge | Not Admissible |
| Do not construct the bridge | Admissible |

---

# Resolution Rule

Both investigations apply the same resolution rule:

Select the admissible candidate that best satisfies the investigation objective according to the reasoning calculus.

The disagreement therefore does not arise from the resolution rule.

---

# Resolution

The disagreement originates from differing interpretations of the shared representations.

Once those interpretations diverge, the remaining reasoning proceeds consistently within each investigation.

---

# Discussion

This example illustrates the primary purpose of FARO.

Rather than asking which conclusion is correct, FARO identifies:

- what the investigators agreed upon,
- where their reasoning first diverged,
- how that divergence propagated through the investigation,
- and why different resolutions resulted.

The disagreement is therefore represented as an explicit feature of the reasoning processes rather than an unexplained conflict.
