# Example — Scientific Investigation

## Purpose

This example demonstrates how Project FAR represents an empirical scientific investigation.

Unlike deductive reasoning, scientific investigations revise conclusions in response to new evidence.

---

# Investigation

## Question

Does fertilizer X increase plant growth?

---

## Objective

Determine whether the available evidence supports the claim that fertilizer X increases plant growth.

---

# Representations

The investigation begins with the following representations.

- Fertilizer X
- Control group of plants
- Experimental group of plants
- Plant height measurements
- Experimental procedure

---

# Representational Structure

The representations are organized into:

- Experimental conditions
- Observations
- Measurements
- Results

The experimental procedure relates the control and experimental groups.

---

# Interpretation

Plant height measurements are interpreted as evidence of growth.

Differences between the control and experimental groups are interpreted as evidence supporting or opposing the hypothesis.

---

# Reasoning Calculus

The investigation employs empirical reasoning using controlled experimentation and evidence evaluation.

---

# Initial Reasoning State

The investigation begins before any measurements have been collected.

The hypothesis remains unconfirmed.

---

# Transformations

## Transformation 1

Conduct the experiment.

---

## Transformation 2

Measure plant growth in both groups.

---

## Transformation 3

Compare the observed results.

---

## Transformation 4

Evaluate whether the observed evidence supports the hypothesis.

Each transformation produces a new reasoning state.

---

# Candidates

The investigation admits two candidates.

- Fertilizer X increases plant growth.
- Fertilizer X does not increase plant growth.

---

# Admissibility Structure (Ω)

| Candidate | Admissibility |
|-----------|---------------|
| Fertilizer X increases plant growth | Admissible |
| Fertilizer X does not increase plant growth | Not Admissible |

The admissibility classifications are determined according to the available evidence and the selected reasoning calculus.

---

# Resolution Rule

Select the admissible candidate best supported by the available evidence.

---

# Resolution

The available evidence supports the conclusion that fertilizer X increases plant growth.

This resolution remains subject to revision if future evidence becomes available.

---

# Discussion

This example illustrates an important distinction between deductive and empirical reasoning.

The structure of the investigation remains unchanged:

- Investigation
- Representations
- Interpretation
- Reasoning Calculus
- Reasoning States
- Transformations
- Candidates
- Admissibility Structure (Ω)
- Resolution Rule
- Resolution

What changes is the reasoning calculus.

Unlike deductive reasoning, empirical reasoning permits future evidence to revise previously accepted resolutions while preserving the same architectural framework.
